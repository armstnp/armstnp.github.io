Expository Teaching and Preaching

Introduction

1 Timothy 4:13,15

We are to be reading, exhorting, and teaching the Word, and the fruit of that study should be evident in us.

Exegesis and Exposition

  • Exegete - to draw out of a text all the truth that's in it.
  • Exposit - expose, make visible or known; crafting a message or lesson.
  • Complementary words

Expositional Preaching

  • Quote: ('What Is Exository Preaching?' Bibliotheca Sacra 131):57
  • Merrill Unger - preserve original meaning of the text in context, and applying it to present-day hearers.
  • Mark Dever - 'takes the main point of a passage of Scripture, makes it the main point of the sermon, and applies it to today.'
  • Pennington - 'in exposition, the preacher reads the text, explains the text in its context, and then applies the text.'

To Be an Expository Sermon…

  • 1) You must begin with a biblical text
  • 2) You must conduct a careful exegesis of that text in order to arrive at the author's original intention
  • 3) You must interpret the text literally and in its context
  • 4) You must prepare and present a message that in a clear and orderly way explains the original intent […]

Perspectives

  • John Calvin

    A preacher misses the mark - if not being presumptuous and blasphemous - if they skew the text away from the author's intent.

  • G. Campbell Morgan on Preaching
    • A biblical sermon contains three elements:
      • Truth - the product of exegesis
      • Clarity - the product of order, explanation, argumentation, illustration, and application
      • Passion - demonstrated in delivery and integrity
    • Usually expository preaching is also systematic - that is, moving section by section through a book of the Bible.
      • Usually guides you towards topical study anyway, and helps present passages in context.

A. Preparation

  • 1. Prepare Yourself
    • a) You must be regenerate. John 8:43 John 10:27 (Fix)
    • b) You must first confess your sin. James 1:21
    • c) With an attitude of humility and dependence, you must pray for illumination. Psalm 119:18,73,125 Ephesians 1:18
    • d) You must commit to do the hard work. 2 Timothy 2:15

      'diligent' - especially conscientious, apply every effort

  • 2. Prepare Your Surroundings
    • a) Have a set time - your best time of day if possible.
    • b) Have a set place - quiet and free of distractions.
    • c) Turn off and shut out all distractions.
    • d) Surround yourself with the necessary resources.
  • 3. Choose a Biblical Book

B. Observation

The heart of exegesis.

Using careful reading, thought, and analysis along with all available tools to systematically study the details of the text in order to arrive at its meaning.

  • The Light of the Reformation
    • Luther: "The Holy Ghost is the all simplest writer…"
    • Calvin: "It is the first business of an interpreter to let the author say what he does say, instead of attributing to him what we think he ought to say."
    • Luther: "First I shake the whole tree, that the ripest [fruit] may fall. Then I climb the tree and shake each limb, and then each branch and then each twig, and then I look under each leaf."
  • What to Observe
    • 1) Context
      • Historical context: the setting of the book in human history.
      • Biblical context: the relationship of the paragraph or section to the surrounding passages, all Scripture.
    • 2) Content
      • Syntax: The relationship of phrases and clauses to one another.
      • Words: the exact sense of the words the author intended.
  • Steps / Tools
    • 1. Remember the Big Picture.
      • The Theme of the Bible
        • Pennington: "God is redeeming a people by His Son, for His Son, to His own glory."
      • The Purpose of the Testaments
        • The OT: He's coming (and why He needs to come)!
        • The NT: He came (Gospels), this is what His coming meant (Acts & epistles), and He's coming again (Revelation).
    • 2. Research the Book's General Introduction.

      Applicable for any book.

      • a) For an overview read the introductions in the MacArthur Study Bible and the ESV Study Bible.

        Helpful, not perfect of course; if you read a couple, they check each other.

      • b) Read the general introductions in your commentaries.

        Tom has 10-15 at hand for Romans, e.g.

    • 3. Read through the Book Multiple Times. (Survey)

      Recommended: 5-30

      Can help to break it into sections.

      Read each time with the 'first time' attitude; can help to read it in different versions.

      • Steps
        • Theme of the book
          • a) Explicit statement Luke 1:1-4 Acts 1:1 John 20:30-31
          • b) Repetition of certain phrases (Genesis - 'these are the generations of')
          • c) Exhortations - usually flow from the purpose (e.g. Hebrews 10:32-35)
        • Setting
        • Author & Audience
        • Date
        • Decide basic book outline
      • Example: Romans
        • Theme: Gospel of God

          Major Sections are tied to this top-level theme

          • Major Section: Gospel Explained 1:18-4:25
            • Minor Section: Guilt of the Pagan
            • Minor Section: Guilt of the Jew
            • Minor Section: Guilt of All Mankind 3:10-20
          • …
          • Major Section: The Gospel Defended 9-11
            • Election
              • Paragraph: …
            • Human Responsibility
            • God's Faithfulness to His People
        • Observe all sections point back to the main theme.
    • 4. Identify the Paragraphs (Prose) or Stanzas (Poetry).

      Geneva Bible added verses to the NT

      Chapter and verse divisions are not inspired; they are utilities for navigation and reference.

      Always ask whether a division is helpful.

      • How?
        • a) Greek and Hebrew Bibles

          Included notes from scholars

        • b) English Translations

          Bolded verse numbers in left-aligned verse numbers are scholarly guesses at paragraph breaks.

          • Prose (either arranged by paragraph or bolded verse number in the left margin)
          • Poetry (either extra return / whitespace or bolded verse number)
        • c) Commentaries
        • d) Your own survey
      • The Footprint of an Expository sermon
        • Overview of a book in a single sermon
        • Section of a book
        • Chapter
        • Paragraph
          • The most common focus of expository preaching.
          • The principle feature of a paragraph is a unifying theme.
        • Sentence or verse
        • Phrase
        • Word or words
        • Topic
          • Either one key passage or more often several carefully exegeted and explained.
    • 5. Analyze the Syntax.
      • A Brief Grammar Lesson
        • The rules by which language communicates meaning
        • Clause - part of a sentence that contains a subject and a verb.
          • 1) Independent - a clause that expresses a complete thought or stands alone; a complete sentence.
          • 2) Dependent - a clause that does not express a complete thought and cannot stand alone; an incomplete sentence.
        • Phrase - a group of words in a sentence without a subject and a verb.
          • 1) Prepositional phrase - a group of words without a verb that is introduced by a preposition.
            • Common prepositions: of, with, about
            • "Anything that a squirrel can be to a stump or an airplane can be to a cloud."
          • 2) Verbal phrase - a group of words without a main verb but with either:
            • a) Participle - a verb form that functions like an adjective.
              • Present participle - verb plus -ing.
              • Past participle - verb plus -ed.
            • b) Gerund - a verb form that functions like a noun.
              • Always: verb plus -ing.
              • 'Waiting_ for a text message kept me glued to my cell phone.'
            • c) Infinitive - "to" plus a verb, used as a noun or adjective.
              • They cannot be made to listen.
              • I need a book to read on holiday.
      • The Key to Analyzing the Grammar
        • 1) Break the text down into smaller units.
        • 2) Identify the main clause - the sentence's subject and verb.
        • 3) Identify all the other phrases and clauses.
        • 4) Understand their relationship to each other.
      • The Best Tool for Analyzing Syntax - Block Diagrams

        Strongest recommendation by Tom Pennington

        • Diagrams the paragraph as a whole.
        • Each phrase and clause is kept in the natural order of the passage.
        • Supporting phrases and clauses are indented under what they modify.
        • The relationships of whole sentences, clauses and phrases are visually apparent at a glance.
        • The Key to Block Diagramming (Ramesh Richard)
          • 1) Identify all possible grammatical or content markers of structure.

            Grammatical Keys indicating structure, and the kind of structure they imply

            'Bob came home from work to rest because he was tired.'

          • 2) Separate major markers from minor markers.

            "You will use your interpretive judgments along with those of others to understand the major and minor divisions of your text."

            'Bob came home from work to rest because he was tired.'

            Because is more important than from and to because it connects two completely different parts of the sentence together.

          • 3) Understand the meaning or the force of the more major markers.

            'Bob came home from work to rest because he was tired.'

            • because carries the meaning of reason.
            • from refers to place.
            • to refers to purpose.
          • 4) Outline the text according to the relative importance of those markers.
        • Example: Ezra 7:10 - 'For_ Ezra had set his heart to study the law of the LORD and to practice it, and to teach His statutes and ordinances in Israel.'

    • 6. Identify a Preliminary Theme.

      Each passage only has one central meaning, or 'single truth intention'.

      Trying to reduce the basic point of the paragraph or section, reduced to a simple sentence.

      Preliminary - study may refine or alter this initial estimation.

      • How?
        • 1. Directly stated

          1 Timothy 4:6-16

          • Theme = 4:16

          Romans 1:16-17,18-23,24-25,26-27,28-32

        • 2. Contained in the words or concepts repeated

          Ephesians 1:3-14 - will or purpose of God

          Romans 2:1-16,17-24,25-29

        • 3. Discerned from the context

          Ephesians 4:25 - Illustration of process of sanctification

          Romans 2:6-11

    • 7. Make Observations and Ask Questions of the Text.

      A continual process during study - probing, pushing, prodding, querying

      • Ask questions of the text as if you had no idea what the text teaches.
        • Who?
          • Wrote it? Said it? Who is the main character? Who is in this account? Written to whom? About whom is it written?
        • What?
          • Major ideas? …
        • Where?
        • When?
        • Why?
          • Why is this important? Why include that detail? Why is so much written about…
        • How?
          • How can it be done? How should it be done? How is the truth illustrated?
      • ii) Make personal observations about what seems to be going on in the text.
        • What do I see in this passage?
          • Key words
          • Key topics
          • Key people
          • Commands
          • Repeated words, topic, phrases
    • 8. Look up All Proper Nouns.
    • 9. Look up Cross References.
      • Parallel passages or cross references that use the same words in the original language.
        • NAU
        • Gospels: Harmony of the Gospels; Thomas & Gundry's A Harmony of the Gospels
      • Passages that contain the same or similar ideas and concepts.
        • Treasury of Scripture Knowledge
        • MacArthur Topical Bible
        • Zondervan Dictionary of Biblical Themes by Manser, McGrath, Packer, & Wiseman
    • 10. Study the Key Words.
      • Why?
        • If words have been in a language for any period of time they acquire a variety of senses.

          e.g. 'The leaders of the company were cool with how the board ran its affairs.'

          'The leaders of the company lost body temperature with how the wood went upstream to spawn its illicit sexual relationships.'

          Context is always king.

      • How to Recognize Key Words
        • 1) Words that play a key role in the passage.
          • Romans 1:16 - ashamed
          • Romans 1:18 - wrath
          • Romans 1:28 - depraved
        • 2) Words that occur frequently in that book or author.
          • 1 John 1 - light
          • Romans 1-4 - righteousness
        • 3) Words that are major biblical words.

          Justified, propitiation, redemption

          Romans 4 - faith and justified

        • 4) Words that could significantly affect the meaning of the passage
      • How?
        • i) Look them up in Greek or Hebrew Lexicons.
        • ii) Do a concordance search.
          • 1) Within the same book.
          • 2) Within other books written by the same author.
          • 3) Within the same biblical chronology.
          • 4) Within all of scripture.
          • What are you looking for?
            • The various senses of the word.
            • The connotation of the word. (e.g. emotional connotation: stubborn, persistent; courageous, …)
            • The usage: literal or figurative.
            • The synonyms used with it
            • The antonyms used with it
        • iii) Based on the context of your paragraph, decide the sense of the word the author intended.
      • The Dangers of Word Studies
        • i) Being misled by the root fallacy.

          e.g. nice -> Latin ignorant

        • ii) Reading all the senses of the word into that one passage.
        • iii) Choosing the sense you like best regardless of the context.
        • iv) Reading the meaning of the English word back into the Greek or Hebrew word.
        • v) Giving a word the exact same sense every time it occurs.
    • 11. Survey the Historical Context.
      • a) Outside sources
        • Secular encyclopedias
        • Bible dictionaries/encyclopedias
        • Other history texts
        • Trustworthy websites
      • b) Scripture itself
    • 12. Establish the Theological Context.
      • a) Identify any significant theological issues in the passage.
      • b) Study those theological concepts in the rest of Scripture and other resources.
      • c) Decide how much of that theological concept must be explained for the passage to be clear.

D. Interpretation

  • Council of Trent on Private Interpretation: "To check unbridled spirits, it [this council] decrees that no one, relying on his own judgment…"

    tl;dr - The magisterium alone had the authority to interpret the Bible.

  • Reformers on Private Interpretation: "Unless I am convinced by the testimony of Scripture and plain reason …"
  • Arguments for Private Interpretation
    • 1) The obligations for faith and obedience are personal, and judgment will be personal
    • 2) Scripture addressed to people, not just leaders
    • 3) People are called upon to study the Scripture personally and to teach it to their children
    • 4) People are praised for …
  • 1. The Importance of Interpretation
    • Only the true meaning of the passage is in fact the Word of God. 2 Peter 3:15-16
  • 2. The Meaning of Interpretation
    • …
  • 3. Three Main Principles of Interpretation
    • a) Interpret based on authorial intent. Matthew 22:29 John 5:39 2 Peter 3:15-16
    • b) Interpret Scripture with Scripture - "The Analogy of Faith"
      • 1st - the total Scripture
      • 2nd - any part of Scripture, such as a verse or passage.
      • Restated: The entire Scripture is the context and guide for [interpreting any other part of Scripture].
    • c) Interpret literally.
      • Follow the normal rules for interpreting any literature: the grammatical-historical method.
      • As with other literature, we must interpret the Bible in the simplest, most literal sense, unless there is indication in the context not to do so.
      • Implications
        • 1) Context rules.
        • 2) Pay attention to the words, syntax, culture, and history.
  • 4. The Dangers of Interpretation

    Drawn from 'How to Interpret the Bible for Yourself', 'How to Read the Bible for all it's Worth'

    • 1) Allegorizing - adding layers of meaning to the text
    • 2) Spiritualizing or moralizing (Song of Solomon)
    • 3) Proof-texting - e.g. 'Judas hanged himself, therefore go and do likewise'
    • 4) Using a narrative as normative - e.g. Gideon's fleece
    • 5) "Nationalizing" - inserting your country into passages
    • 6) Cultural back-loading - placing current culture back into the text, e.g. evolution, homosexuality, role of marriage, etc.
    • 7) Literalizing - Making a figure of speech literal, e.g. communion
    • 8) Dogmatizing - Forcing a passage to meet your religious system
