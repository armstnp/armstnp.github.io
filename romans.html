      • 1c Personal: Paul's own conversion to Christ

        Benjamin - the only son of Jacob born in the Promised Land; Jerusalem resided in its territory.

        Judah - the tribe of David - and Benjamin were both in the faithful south of Israel after its schism.

        Paul doesn't seem to make his point out of personal desire, but rather pointing out that as a Jewish Christian, he is proof that God has not wholly rejected His people.

        Indeed, Paul was the prime exemplar of this, given who he was before he was saved by God. 1 Timothy 1:13-16

      • 2a Biblical: God's OT promises not to forsake His people

        The wording of the initial question and his response here come directly from the OT: 1 Samuel 12:19-22 Psalm 94:14 Jeremiah 31:35-37

        Paul is refuting the idea that God has violated His promise to Israel.

        Central to God's nature is His inability to lie, to act contrary to His own nature: Numbers 23:19 Titus 1:2 Hebrews 6:18

        The ethnic descendants of Israel are still the special objects of God's care.

        There is therefore no place for anti-semitism in the heart of the believer, both because they are made in the image of God, and because of this special care He holds.

        We can also trust God will keep His promises to you. He is never undependable; He has never lied, never failed, and never will. He is incapable of doing so.

      • 2a Theological: God's electing love of the nation

        'To-know before' , from

        This must be referring to the entire nation of Israel:

        • It best fits the immediate context from 1
        • It best fits the entire context of chapter 11
        • Election of the nation is in view Romans 11:28

        Amos 3:2

        9:4-5 Through Israel, He brought the Messiah

        Deuteronomy 7:7-8;10:14-15;32:8-9

        Human sin cannot cancel out the promises of God. 3:3-4

        8:29 Just as He foreknew Israel, He foreknows those He saves, and just as He never will reject Israel, He will never reject you, believer.

      • 2b-4 Biblical: God's response to Elijah

        9:27 introduces the idea of a 'remnant' from Isaiah.

        In OT it refers to a spiritual remnant who are saved; embodies both judgment and hope.

        In chapter 9, 'remnant' is used negatively; here, it is used positively.

        Paul makes clear here that the idea is not a new one.

        'appeals' or 'pleads' - to intercede on behalf of someone. 8:27,34 But here, it's used in a negative way, against Israel.

        After Solomon's death, his son Rehoboam was foolish and the kingdom was divided; Judah and Benjamin in the south ('Judah'), and the other ten tribes in the north ('Israel').

        Jeroboam created their own custom religion in the north as a political ploy to prevent people from having to travel to Judah to worship. One place of worship was in Dan and Bethel, on the north and south ends of the kingdom. Gold images of calves are placed at these worship centers; these weren't images of God Himself, he said, but just things you could worship God through, or that He stood on, etc. This syncretist ploy tried to marry pagan Ba'al worship to worship of the true God, and He was condemned as it became a regular practice in the land.

        Over time, the worship refocused to worship Ba'al, and within 50 years after Solomon, under the reign of King Ahab son of Omri, the evil grew exceedingly great.

        1 Kings 16:25-19:14

        'Ethbaal' - 'Ba'al is alive', a priest of Ba'al, who murdered his predecessor.

        'Asherah' - the consort of Ba'al

        • 3a An Accurate Assessment

          Elijah's diagnosis of the state of Israel is almost entirely accurate: his spiritual diagnosis of the worship of Ba'al was accurate.

          'demolished' - razed, total intolerance of worship of Yahweh.

        • 3b A Mistaken Conclusion

          Elijah magnified the pain of his circumstances and minimized God's power, bringing him to depression and willingness to forsake his own life.

          • He has always had a remnant.

          • The true believing remnant doesn't bow to Ba'al.

          • 1. God has not forsaken Israel.
          • 2. God is saving more Jewish people than we think.
          • 3. God always has a larger remnant than we think.
          • 4. The spiritual majority are never right.

          • 5. God most often accomplishes His purposes in quiet and seemingly insignificant ways, rather than dramatic ones.

            Christ's church will be built.

    • 4. 5-6 The Implications
      • a) 5a There still exists a believing remnant.

        Again, 'I have kept for myself…' in 4 and 'according to the choice of grace' in 5.

        'choice' - election, seen and discussed earlier in 11

      • c) 5b God's choice of the remnant is by grace alone.

        Tom's considerations:

        • Paul was overwhelmed with this doctrine, and consistently returns to it, as he loved it personally.
        • Paul knew how difficult it was for us merit-based people to accept grace without our own works added to it.

        He makes the point that grace and works are mutually exclusive as means for salvation.

        'no longer' - does not imply that there used to be a different means, but rather that the logical argument can no longer be maintained for works as a means.

        God's choice excludes anything a human does, good or evil; it is impossible for man to in any way influence or affect God's choice, lest it not be God's choice at all, and therefore no longer be grace.

        To bring his primary point back home, God has not abandoned His people, and nothing they have done can or will subvert His free giving of grace to the remnant He selected.

    • 2. 7b The Elect in Israel Have Obtained Righteousness

      9:27;10:16 Only a remnant

      9:11-12,15-16,18,29 Sovereign election is the cause

      'but the election obtained it' - Paul does not call them 'the elect', to avoid the idea that the elect were elected by inherent merit.

      1 Thessalonians 1:4

      Paul brings us back to the grace of God again.

    • 3. 7c-10 The Rest of Israel Were Hardened
      • a. 7c Stated & Defined

        'hardened' - in secular Greek, used to define medical conditions, like calcification of a gall stone or around a healing broken bone.

        2 Corinthians 3:14 A hardened mind, difficulty comprehending the truth.

        Here, Paul describes a hardened heart, as well; a stubborn, resistant heart.

        'were hardened' - passive tense; in context, the actor is God Himself. Romans 9:18;11:8

        Hardening is a judicial act of God, based on the rebellion or unbelief of the sinner. This is a judgment we all deserve.

        Note that God does not actively produce evil, sin, or unbelief in the heart. He does not tempt any man. Instead, He simply passes them by and withholds the softening effects of His Spirit, allowing them to have the results of what they want: their sin. It permanently binds people in the sin they have chosen for themselves.

        The sinner begins by hardening his own heart against God, and it ends in God judicially hardening the sinner's heart.

        Compare to the relationship between the sun and Texas clay. The sun hardens the clay, not directly, but rather by acting on inherent properties of the clay itself (see in contrast what the sun does to wax). Similarly, God allows the inherent properties of the sinner to take hold.

        Compare also to an imprisoned criminal; this often makes them not repentant, but a hardened criminal. The jail doesn't harden them, but it reinforces the existing properties.

        Hebrews 3:13 If you do not respond to the Word, the more you hear it, the harder your heart becomes. Hardening your heart to the truth is deadly serious.

        Nehemiah 9:16-17,26-31 This is a mark of Israel's history.

        Ephesians 4:17-18 The same happens for every unbeliever.

      • b. 8-10 Defended

        He chooses three passages: from the Law, the Prophets, and the (poetic writing?)

        • Isaiah 29:10

          'stupor' - total lack of spiritual sensitivity, as one blind drunk; self-induced

        • Deuteronomy 29:4
        • Psalm 69:22-23

          An imprecatory Psalm; not vengeful, but rather a call for God's justice on those who will not turn to Him out of a concern for God's glory and God's people. Here it represents a righteous man being attacked by his enemies, and Paul uses it to point to Christ and His eventual vindication by God.

          The spiritual advantages given to them would become their downfall instead.

          'retribution' - repayment, usually for punishment that a sin deserves. This is divine justice enacted, not capricious. Lamentations 3:64-66

    • Lessons from this passage
      • 1. 33-34 A lesson in submitting to God's Wisdom.

        Do not argue or question God or throw His word aside to come up with your own plan.

        Let God be God. Wrestle with it, but don't compromise a high view of God in the process.

      • 2. A lesson in anticipating God's justice.
        • If you are not a believer in Jesus Christ, this is a sobering reminder that God is perfect in His justice - toward you - and a call to repentance and faith. The longer you wait to submit, the harder it will become. I plead for you in sorrow to accept God's grace now, today.
        • If you are a believer and your heart is often heavy over all the injustice in the world, this passage should encourage you that one day justice will come.

II. 11-32 Israel's Future Salvation

Four Basic Propositions

  1. 11-12 As a whole, Israel has rejected her Messiah and His gospel.
  2. 11-12a Israel's rejection of Jesus Christ has resulted in the salvation of the Gentiles.

3a. 14 The salvation of the Gentiles is currently leading to the salvation of a few from Israel… 3b. 15,25-26 …but will eventually lead to the ultimate salvation of all Israel.

  1. 12b The ultimate salvation of all Israel will produce even greater blessing for the world.

    • 3. Some Basic Definitions of the Gifts

      'Understanding Spiritual Gifts' - Dr. Robert Thomas

      Temporary sign gifts

      1. Apostle
      An office in the early church of Jesus' official representatives through whom God provided inspired, authoritative revelation.
      • Had to be personally appointed by Christ; Paul stated he was the last apostle.
      2. Prophecy
      An ability to be a channel of direct revelation primarily in local churches, which was crucial until the completion of the NT. Acts 13:1; 15:32
      3. Distinguishing (or discerning) of spirits
      An ability to pass immediate judgment on the validity of prophecy given in the assembly.
      4. Word of wisdom
      An ability to receive and pass on to others a revelation of the mysteries of God. Private as opposed to congregational.
      5. Word of knowledge
      An ability to grasp the content of special revelation connected with the word of wisdom, to systematize it, and extend its implications to new situations.

      Temporary sign gifts - Confirmation

      6. Miracles
      The ability to perform supernatural acts such as raising the dead Acts 9:40 and blinding Elymas Acts 13:8-11
      7. Healings
      A supernatural ability to physically cure illnesses and congenital issues, not indiscriminately but when the person was conscious it was God's will.
      8. Faith
      An ability to believe God to the point that He would miraculously remove hindrances in the spread of the gospel.
      9. Tongues
      An ability to speak a foreign language not learned by normal human methods. Acts 2:4-11
      10. Interpretation of tongues
      An ability to translate into your own language from a foreign language not learned by normal human methods.

      Permanent Edifying Gifts - Teaching

      11. Teaching
      An ability to grasp, arrange, and present revealed truth effectively and in an organized manner, so those who hear gain an enhanced understanding of Scripture, either publicly Acts 18:11 or privately Acts 18:26.
      12. Exhortation
      An ability to explain and apply the truth to believers and persuade them to believe and obey it.
      • 1 Thessalonians 2:11 In 2 Timothy 4:2, exhortation is seen to be needed when using the gift of teaching. Interestingly, often coupled with music, in which we exhort one another with the truths being sung.
      13. Evangelism
      An ability to persuade lost people to put their trust in Christ, either publicly Acts 8:13,25 or personally Acts 8:35
      14. Pastor-teacher
      An ability to teach and to shepherd God's people, primarily exercised as an elder in the local church.

      Permanent Edifying Gifts - Serving

      15. Service/Helps
      An ability to know how best to meet people's temporal needs and an unusual skill in meeting those needs. Acts 6:1-6; 9:39
      16. Giving
      An ability to effectively invest one's material resources for spiritual and kingdom purposes.
      • Barnabas, Acts 4
      17. Leading/Administration
      An ability in leadership and administration.
      • Essential for elders.
      18. Showing Mercy
      An ability to help, assist, and relieve those experiencing distress, misery, or pain. Acts 9:36

14:1-15:13 Navigating Christian Liberty

The Nature of Conscience

  • Issues on which Christians Disagree

    Each issue has moral aspects to which the Bible speaks; but just because the Bible speaks to those elements, that does not mean it is black and white.

    God has provided us two tools: the conscience, and His eternal Word. He gives the tools that we are to use to make decisions in two passages: Romans 14, and 1 Corinthians 8-10 (more or less).

    Note that Paul deals with the same issues in both passages, but only refers to the conscience in Corinthians.

    • 1. The drinking of alcoholic beverages
    • 2. The style of worship music
    • 3. The education of our children
    • 4. Parenting philosophies
    • 5. Entertainment choices
    • 6. The selection of a spouse
    • 7. Clothing choices
    • 8. The use of slang and vulgarities
    • 9. The response to COVID
    • 10. A variety of political issues
  • I. What is Conscience?
    • Gr. word means lit. 'to know together with'
    • Evangelical Dictionary of Theology

      'That faculty by which one distinguishes between the morally right and wrong, which urges one to do that which he recognizes to be right and restrains him from doing that which he recognizes to be wrong, which passes judgment on his acts and executes that judgment within the soul.'

  • II. How does Conscience Work?

    Latin word means lit. 'with knowledge'

    The conscience works with knowledge: this means conscience is only as effective as the knowledge that it has!

    • Romans 14:14 'I know and am convinced that nothing is unclean […]'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:1 'We all have knowledge'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:2 'If anyone supposes that he knows anything […]'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:3 'We know that […]'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:7 'Not all men have this knowledge.'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:10 'You who have knowledge […]'
    • 1 Corinthians 8:11 'Through your knowledge'
    • Seen visibly in Romans 2: those who have and do not have the Law.

      Everyone who has a soul has the 'hardware' of the conscience, pre-loaded with 'software': the basic requirements of God's Law, a 'courtroom inside the soul', measuring your every choice against its understanding of God's Law.

      The sentence of the conscience is regret, remorse, and profound guilt.

      This also means that we tend to add bad information to our conscience over time; either flawed, or - more likely - flat-out wrong. You are continually bombarded with information that informs your conscience: first from your parents, then your peers 1 Corinthians 15:33 and your teachers, then books, web, social media; all ideas we consume influence and shape our consciences.

      But our greatest problem is our own flawed thinking: when we feel guilt, we begin - through our own flawed thinking - to refuse and try to suppress and retrain our conscience. 'It's not really bad; it won't hurt anyone; I deserve this;' etc.

      This can produce an insensitive conscience: feeling no guilt for doing evil things. 1 Timothy 4:2 Ephesians 4:19 This can allow you to redefine your conscience: convincing yourself that little white lies aren't wrong, or that stealing is fine because 'you're entitled to it'.

      This can also produce an oversensitive conscience (called a 'weak conscience' by Paul). As an example, if you grew up in a home that said it was wrong to wear pink shirts, that has informed your conscience; and now, when you wear pink shirts, you feel guilty. It's not a rightly informed conscience; the consicience is only as good as the info it's trained with. It's a warning light that responds as it's been programmed. Ignoring that warning light may damage your soul, or even destroy it - but it must be properly informed by God's Word.

  • III. What Is an 'Issue of Conscience'?
    • A moral decision is an issue of conscience if it meets two clear biblical tests:
      • 1. No clear biblical command (chapter and verse)

        'Thus sayeth the Lord: thou shalt […]'

      • 2. No clear biblical prohibition (chapter and verse)

        'Thus sayeth the Lord: thou shalt not […]'

      • Romans 14:1,14,22 1 Corinthians 8:8; 10:23

        'all things are lawful' - not that all moral choices are allowed, but within the bounds of God's law, you have liberty.

  • Several Major Implications
    • A. Enjoy your Christian liberty.

      There is great freedom in this liberty! We are not bound by any standard but the Word of God.

      1 Corinthians 4:6 'Do not exceed what has been written.'

      The sole caveat: follow the conscience of those in authority over you, especially children under their parents.

    • B. Educate your conscience.

      Your conscience can be unintentionally either insensitive or oversensitive. Don't ignore it, but rather evaluate it against God's Word.

    • C. Follow the principles of Christian liberty.

      Christian liberty is limited, and the Christian is to limit the exercise of their liberty; this is the primary focus of these passages.

      • 1. Never allow your Christian liberty to cause disunity. 14:1-12, 15:5-13
      • 2. Never allow your Christian liberty to cause others to sin. 14:13-21 cf. 1 Corinthians 8:1-13
      • 3. Never allow your Christian liberty to cause you to sin…
        • a. By violating your conscience. 14:22-23
        • b. By using your liberty as an excuse for sin. 1 Corinthians 9:24-10:22
        • c. By allowing your liberty to become all about you…
          • Rather than fellow Christians. 15:1-4 1 Corinthians 10:23-26
          • Rather than unbelievers. 1 Corinthians 9:1-23; 10:27-33
          • Rather than God's glory. 1 Corinthians 10:31
    • D. Don't ignore your conscience.

      It is a gift from God; it's not perfect, it's flawed, but thank God that it still functions, and respond as the Scriptures instruct.

      The guilt you feel from your conscience is a faint echo of the eventual, ultimate judgment of God. Don't ignore that warning of judgment, or it will lead to disaster. Like pilots ignoring the warning cry of an airplane to 'pull up!', disaster will come if we consistently do what our consciences tell us is wrong.

      Our response must be to repent, turn from what we know to be sin, and to turn to Christ. This is the only way to avoid that future condemnation.

    • 3. Don't argue with a fellow Christian about issues of conscience, but gently, graciously educate his conscience with Scripture.

IV. 14:22-23 Never Allow your Liberty to Cause You to Sin

  • A. 22-23 By Violating your Own Conscience

    'The faith which you have' - you who have strong faith, i.e. the stronger brother in Paul's ongoing comparison.

    'have as your own conviction before God' - keep it between you and God; not that you can never speak of it (cf. 14) in appropriate contexts, but never in the context of a debate or argument, in a manner of condemnation or imposition.

    'Happy is the one who does not condemn himself in what he approves.' - there is joy to be found in the liberty we have to not fear much of what God has made!

    'But the one who doubts is condemned if he eats' - if a weak brother eats in spite of his doubts, he is condemned: not by himself, but by God, as Paul has only used this word 'condemn' regarding God. Why is this?

    'because his eating is not from faith; and whatever is not from faith is sin.' - 'faith' here refers to a conviction regarding sin. If you are sincerely convicted that God has prohibited something, and yet you do it anyway, even if that thing is not inherently contrary to God's will, it is an act of rebellion: you are intentionally acting contrary to what you believe God wants you to do.

    This is an action that is made by someone who is persuaded from Scripture that something is sinful for them. The heart attitude is in view. As a weak brother matures, their conscience should grow into understanding what God does and does not command; this retraining of the conscience takes time, and false positives and false negatives alike can pose problems as that process takes place.

    How do you retrain the conscience?

    1. Meditate on those biblical texts that focus on the sufficiency of Scripture. The Bible is enough; you don't need to go beyond it.
    2. Study what the Scripture says about Christian liberty.
    3. Study what the Scripture says about the issue of conscience that troubles you.

    Note that the issue is not emotions, but knowledge: the basis for your conscience. Exercising Christian liberty when your mind is certain it is okay can still cause your emotions to bring feelings of guilt and warning. Be careful not to confuse the two.

I. 14-16 The Timeless Spiritual Purpose: to Remind All Believers of the Gospel We have Believed

  • A. 14 It was Written to Christians.

    'my brethren' - used of both men and women

    'I myself also am convinced' - the Grk. tense says he has come and will always hold to this conclusion; no doubt in his mind that they were genuine believers in Christ. This letter wasn't to evangelize and inform them, though God has used it to that end through the ages. cf. 1:8

    Paul was responding to the specific strengths and challenges of the Roman church from Priscilla and Aquila. He knew they were not just believers, but mature believers. Paul picks out those marks of maturity here: 'full of goodness, filled with all knowledge, and able to admonish one another.'

    'goodness' - a moral quality especially characterized by interest in the good of others.

    'full of goodness' - not merely fleetingly, but saturated with it. This is an outflow of their knowledge in their saving faith.

    'all knowledge' - not perfect knowledge of all things, but rather love for and understanding of all the broad elements of the faith; and the Romans were clearly not shallow, either.

    'able to admonish one another' - they had the capacity to counsel, warn, and instruct one another, to avoid or stop dangerous paths in one anothers' lives.

    Spiritual maturity is marked by a lack of preoccupation with yourself, and instead looking to the needs of others. Lack of these things is a mark of a lack of genuine faith; it is a test by which we can measure our faith and extent of maturity.

  • B. 15a It was Written as a Reminder.

    Paul is writing to a church he did not found and has not visited, so he tries here to humbly ensure they don't misunderstand his intent.

    'so as to remind you again' - 'remind' only occurs in this form here in the NT; to bring to mind, to recall. This should all be recalling that faith they already held in common.

    cf. 1:15 Time and again in Scripture, God's people are told to remind themselves, to remember, calling these truths to mind:

    • God's person Psalm 42:5-6
    • God's past actions, towards others and yourself Deuteronomy 5:15
    • God's commands Psalm 103:17-18

    Recall the feasts of Israel, the weekly Sabbath, the ordinances of Baptism and the Lord's Supper; all were created to remind God's people of God and His works. In a similar way, the epistles were written not just to inform us of what we don't know, but to remind us of what we do. 2 Peter 1:12-15

    • Of the basic truths of the gospel
      • God is a Righteous Creator
      • Man is a Rebellious Sinner
      • Jesus is the Only Savior
      • We must Repent and Believe in Jesus as Savior and Lord
    • Of the fullness of the gospel

      This book expands on the implications of the Gospel: the extent of human depravity, the reason we are justified; election, security, the role of Israel, etc.

    • Of the practical application of the gospel

      Renewing your mind, serving and loving the church, obeying earthly authority, using liberty wisely, etc.

  • C. 15b-16b It was Written under Divine Authority

    Paul's authority did not stem from any deserving reason from himself, but rather because God had elected and called him to apostleship.

    Paul's role as given by God was to minister to the Gentiles. Romans 1:5; 11:13 Acts 9:15 Galatians 2:2 Ephesians 3:8

    Paul is not the authority; he is merely the appointed messenger. This letter is from Christ, a reminder directly from Him to us.

    'a minister of Christ' - used in this way of OT priests.

    'offering' - only here and Ephesians 5:2; this analogy shows that Paul thinks of the gospel as the means by which the Gentile church is made an offering - pleasing to God. Isaiah 66:20

    This offering is 'acceptable' - God would only accept that which was sanctified to Him, and likewise, Gentiles needed to be made clean by the Gospel to make them an acceptable sacrifice.

    Note that this is not the establishment of an ongoing priestly order.

II. 17-33 The Timely Ministry Purpose: to Prepare Roman Believers for the Visit He Had Planned

While it's historical in nature, there are still lessons we can learn about our own service to Christ.

  • A. 17-19 His Former Ministry Accomplishments: Jerusalem to Eastern Europe
    • 1. 17-18a Christ is the Sole Reason for our Accomplishments

      Paul does not boast in his own achievements; he boasts only in Christ, who is the one who accomplished his ministry work.

      'I will not presume' - lit. 'I would not be so bold'; the same word he used in v15.

      Paul emphasizes that he himself is not responsible for anything in his ministry; but rather Christ accomplished all, using Paul as the instrument. Christ alone builds His church. All eternal results are Christ's work, not ours - and this is what the rest of this epistle has already revealed and stated.

    • 2. 18b Christ Set the Spiritual Goal for our Efforts

      Seen also in 1:5; 16:26; the same point is made in all three: the only correct response to the Gospel for all men (and more specificially the Gentiles he's writing to) is obedience. This is obedience to the announcement of the Gospel, the invitation of the Gospel, and the command of the Gospel: Hear this good news; come to Christ and be saved; repent and believe, lest you perish. Failure to obey only adds to your guilt.

      This isn't just obedience to the Gospel, but also subsequent obedience to God's Word. 'Make disciples, and teach them to observe all that I taught.'

      Paul's words and miracles served the same purpose: to enact this end. Christ's goal for our ministry is likewise Christ-focused, not self-focused. We are to bring others to obey Jesus Christ: the obedience of faith.

    • 3. 19a Christ Provides the Spiritual Gifts for our Assignment

      'signs and wonders' - miraculous events; 'signs' refers to their purpose, and 'wonders' describes their effects. This idiom is used throughout Scripture, and throughout Acts for works of Jesus and the Apostles alike.

      Three times in history when there were miracle-working men: Moses-Joshua, Elijah-Elisha, and Christ and His apostles; each period being around 65-70 years in duration. God performed miraculous wonders Himself throughout history, but these are the only periods in which He worked these wonders through men.

      The primary purpose of miracles has always been to establish the credentials of someone who claims to speak for God. cf. John 5:36; 10:24-25,37-38; 20:30-31 Acts 2:22; 14:3 Hebrews 2:3-4 2 Corinthians 12:12

      For us - who are not apostles, and do not need the signs and wonders of apostolic credentials - we are yet given gifts to serve in the role God has prepared for us to serve within the church. He will equip you to serve.

    • 4. 19b Christ Determines the Precise Place for our Service

      Paul describes the breadth of his ministry, reaching north of Achaia and Macedonia; between the gap when he left Ephesus to Jerusalem, perhaps, in Acts 20:1-2.

      'all around as far as' - lit. a ring around, and you can see where he made a loop around the Aegean Sea and the Mediterranean. This period of around 25 years - writing this letter from Corinth, near the end of the third missionary journey - he is summarizing his entire ministry thus far. Asia Minor, Macedonia, and Greece - eastern Europe - was the region he covered.

      Paul had desired to go into Asia, but 'the Spirit prevented him', as recorded in Acts 16:6. Christ determines where you will minister. cf. v32 1 Corinthians 12

    • 5. 19c Christ Decides the Single Measurement of our Success

      lit. 'I have fulfilled the Gospel of Christ' - to completely and thoroughly accomplish something. How could he have completed the preaching of the Gospel? How does this reflect the relatively small number of believers and churches his ministry produced? His focus was not on numbers, but rather on his strategic placement and planting of churches in key cities and with sufficient maturity that the church would continue to grow in his absence.

      Paul had been faithful to carry out the task Christ had assigned him to do: to carry the Gospel to those regions. Christ's measure was based not on results, but on faithfulness. After all, if Christ accomplishes the results, why would God measure you by what He accomplishes? Rather, he measures you on faithfulness alone.

        Sometimes God says, 'Yes,' entirely differently than we could have imagined. Paul was rescued from his enemies in Jerusalem by being arrested by the Romans. God brought Paul to Rome after fleeing public stoning, imprisoned for years, suffering shipwreck, and so on.

        Sometimes, God answers, 'No,' forever. 2 Corinthians 12:7-9

        And sometimes, 'No,' for now. Romans 1:10

16:1-24 Personal Greetings to Friends

I. 1-16 The Apostle's Greeting to the Roman Christians

  • A. 1-2 Commendation of the Letter's Carrier

    1 Timothy 3 Not deaconesses, not considered part of the laity; but rather wives of deacons.

    'church' - 'ecclesia', used for the first time in this book!

    'Cenchrea' - the port city of Corinth, from which Paul wrote this letter.

    This book, which has so vastly influenced so many generations of the church, was carried by Phoebe, whose name means 'bright'.

    Likely someone of generous means and wealth, given she's traveling alone (and would therefore have attendants with her).

  • B. 3-15 Greeting of the Roman Christians
    • 3-5a Prisca and Aquila

      'Prisca' - the shortened form of her full name, Priscilla

      'Aquila' - derived from the latin word for 'eagle'

      Acts 18:1-5,18-19 Paul stayed with them for 18 months at Corinth, then they traveled with him to Ephesus where they remained.

      1 Corinthians mentions the church they hosted at their home in Ephesus.

      They returned to Rome after Claudius died and his edict barring Jews from Rome had been lifted.

      Paul gives thanks not only for their faithfulness and care of him, but also for the result in the growth of the church among the Gentiles because of their defense of him.

    • 5b-13 Other Individuals in the Roman Churches

      Epaenetus was clearly special to Paul, an encouraging firstfruit.

      Andronicus and Junius - likely husband and wife. 'the apostles' refers clearly to the twelve Apostles, and Paul is indicating this couple was esteemed among them. This couple was saved early on in the church!

      Ampliatus was likely a slave or freed slave, as was Urbanus; the latter may have even been a part of Caesar's household.

      Apelles was well-known and a respected believer.

      Aristobulus does not seem to be a believer, but many among his sons clearly were. If he was a grandson of Herod the Great, he would have been dead 6-7 years and the slaves would have been transferred to the Emperor's staff.

      Herodion would have been a Jew serving one of the Herodian family.

      Narcissus was another prominent Roman household, notorious in Roman history. A former slave who was under the Emperor Claudius; an evil man who committed suicide shortly before Paul wrote this letter.

      Tryphaena and Tryphosa - typically names of slaves, and likely sisters, or even twins. The names mean 'delicate' and 'dainty', yet Paul marks that they worked to exhaustion in the Lord.

      Persis - likely a female slave from Persia. 'has worked hard' - may be elderly and no longer able to serve.

      Rufus - latin for 'red'. Mark 15:21 refers to this man, whose father had borne Jesus' cross to Golgotha: Simon of Cyrene! Mark wrote his Gospel in Rome, so he is connecting the account to Roman believers they would know. Rufus' mother had apparently treated Paul as her own son, and he greets her as well.

    • 14-15 Key Roman Churches and their Leaders

      'Asyncritus' - lit. incomparable

      'Phlegon' - 'burning', a common dog's name

      'and the brothers and sisters with them' - the above may be the elders of those churches, then.

      'Philologus' - lit. 'lover of words'; Julia likely his wife. Nereus and his sister may have been their children.

  • C. 16a Greeting One Another

    This command comes five times in the NT. What does it mean to 'greet one another with a holy kiss'?

    In the first century, this was done as an expression of love for family, or for an expression of affection for a very close friend. This lasted for several centuries (Justin Martyr spoke of it in his writings as customary).

    The lesson behind the action is preserved, even if the culture has shifted. We are to show the same affection we show towards close family towards fellow believers as well, in appropriate ways. How would you greet family you haven't seen in a long time, and may not see again for a while? This is the kind of greeting in view; nothing impure.

  • Abiding Lessons
    • 1. Christ intended His church to be diverse.

      In these verses, Paul mentions:

      • 26 individuals, two households, three or more churches
      • Seventeen men, half of which are probably elders; and nine women
      • Three married couples
      • Some married to unbelievers, and/or widows
      • Most names are Gentile, and a few are Jewish; several different ethnicities and nationalities are in view
      • Ten of these names are names given to slaves
      • Four of these names are given to people of higher social standing and wealth
      • Conflicting religious and cultural backgrounds
      • Believers old and new
      • Roman natives, and believers from across the empire

      How could people from such different backgrounds enjoy such unity?

    • 2. The source of our unity is Christ our Lord.

      Nine times in these 16 verses, Paul says they are 'in the Lord' or 'in Christ'.

      1 Corinthians 12:13

    • 3. True Christians love other believers and forge interconnecting relationships across the world.

      The Christian community is a small community, and we should forge those relationships among all we know.

      Do you love other believers? If you don't, there is something desperately wrong with your profession of faith. 1 John

    • 4. Christ cares about every believer and knows each by name!

      Isaiah 43:1,7

II. 17-20 A Serious Warning about False Teachers

  • A. 17a The Perpetual Danger of False Teachers

    Christ warned of three great dangers in the Sermon on the Mount:

    • Matthew 7:13-14 Entering through the wrong gate; any gate but the truth leads to destruction
    • Matthew 7:21-27 Making a false profession of faith
    • Matthew 7:15-20 Listening to false prophets (one of the primary ways people enter the wrong gate)

    Paul gives an exhortation, a plea that comes with apostolic authority.

    'keep your eye on' - a Greek word that means to keep close track of, 'scopeo'; scrutinize, look out for. This is in the present continuous tense: 'continually be on watch for'

    Matthew 7:15 'beware' - a state of alert against danger, also in the present tense: remain constantly on alert against error.

    All of these make clear that this is a real danger that is continuously present.

    Deuteronomy 13,18 contains Moses' warnings that there would always be false prophets. Numbers 22-24 shows the first such recorded one. Throughout the OT, false prophets would tell the people the opposite of what God would say, and rather would tell the people what they wanted to hear, to their own profit.

    In the NT, you see the Pharisees' false teaching damning their followers; and in near every church Paul wrote to, he confronted false teaching within that church. And we can only expect further false teachers 'til the end of the church age. These warnings apply to us today as much as it ever has.

    False teachers look like genuine believers, at least on the surface. They pass themselves off as genuine. 2 Corinthians 11:13-15 Matthew 7:15 They use the language of Christianity and Scripture, but distort or replace their meaning. These are predators on the flock of Christ, using and destroying the sheep for their own lusts.

    Some false teachers are complete charlatans and hucksters, knowing they are lying frauds. Others deceive others while themselves being deceived by Satan: a lineage of error. 2 Timothy 3:13

    • 1. "Those who cause dissensions"

      'dissensions' - linked with 'disputes' and 'factions' elsewhere in Scripture; a state of divisive opposition, convincing people that they've believed the wrong thing.

    • 2. "Those who cause […] hinderances"

      Grk. 'skandelon' - the bait stick that tripped a trap; the teaching or actions by a teacher that trap others, tempting them to act contrary to Scripture.

    • 3. "Those who teach [or act] contrary to the teaching which you learned"

      1 Corinthians 1 Truth can be a stumbling block to people. The real test of a false teacher: is it contrary to the clear meaning of Scripture? Is their life and teaching contrary to the Gospel which has been passed down?

      2 Timothy 3:8ff

    • Main Categories of False Teachers
      • a. Deuteronomy 13:1-5 Those who teach the worship of false gods
      • b. Those who claim the true God but teach some combination of the following errors:
        • i. A false source of authority that contradicts God's previous revelation

          Deuteronomy 13:4-5 Isaiah 8:20 1 John 4:6 2 John 9-10

        • ii. A false Christ or a false gospel

          2 John 9 Galatians 1:8-9

        • iii. A lifestyle of license as opposed to personal holiness

          Jeremiah 23:14,17 cf. Ephesians 5:6 Revelation 2:20-23

        • iv. Legalism and asceticism as opposed to personal holiness and the fruit of the Spirit

          Matthew 23:13-23 Colossians 2:21-22 1 Timothy 4:1-3 Appealing to man's pride.

        • v. What people want to hear as opposed to God's truth

          Jeremiah 23:16-18,21-22 Micah 3:5 2 Timothy 4:3-4 They give false security, emphasizing the love of God, avoiding the holiness, righteousness, justice, and wrath of God. Promoting the value of man, but not the sinfulness of man.

    • Modern False Teachers
      • Pope Francis (and the entire Catholic church)
      • Cults (Mormonism, Jehovah's Witnesses)
      • The Church of Christ (baptismal regeneration)
      • Postmodern Teachers
      • Faith Healers and Word of Faith teachers (Prosperity Gospel)
      • Academics who use slick talk to undermine Scripture while claiming its authority
    • Caveat

      If you are a true believer, a false teacher will never lead you into a damning error. Matthew 24:24

      But they can thwart your spiritual growth. Ephesians 4:14 2 Peter 3:17-18

      Take great care not to propagate their error into the lives of others.

  • B. 17 The Biblical Response to False Teachers
    • 1. Measure all teachers against Scripture.

      'contrary to the teaching which you learned'

      Isaiah 8:20 Acts 17:11

    • 2. Watch out for teachers who deviate from Scripture.

      'keep your eye on those' - pay careful attention to, look out for; eventually, a false teacher is revealed by his fruit.

      • Three kinds of fruit to help identify false teachers:
        • a. Matthew 7:15-20 Character and Conduct

          'A wolf can wear sheep's clothing, but it cannot grow a sheep's coat.'

          This is in a passage about true and false disciples of Christ.

          2 Timothy 3:5

        • b. Luke 6:43-45 Content

          1 John 4 gives criteria for Biblical teaching:

          • Does the teaching exalt the biblical Jesus and the biblical Gospel?
            • Sin, depravity, God's coming judgment, eternal destruction
            • Repentance and faith
          • Does the teaching promote true holiness?
          • Does it exalt the Word of God, or does it promote the teacher's own ideas and mind?
          • Does it promote love for God and love for others, or self-love, self-fulfillment, and self- satisfaction?
        • c. Luke 6:40 2 Timothy 2:16 2 Peter 2:2 Revelation 2:20 Converts
    • 3. Avoid all teachers who deviate from Scripture.

      'keep away from' / 'steer clear of' / 'avoid' - treat them as completely outside the Christian faith. Do not engage with them in any regard. Be gracious, but don't give any hint of approval. Even 'debating' them can lend them credence. 2 Timothy 3:5 2 John 7-11

    • 1. Their Spiritual Condition - Dead

    • 2. Their Real Motive - Their Own Appetites

      A self-centered lifestyle, motivated by their own lusts. Philippians 3:19 2 Peter 2:13b-15a Jude 4

    • 3. Their Deceptive Method - Smooth, Flattering Speech

      False eloquence, flattery, rhetorical flourishes. Colossians 2:4 2 Peter 2:1,3

    • 4. Their Favorite Targets - The Unsuspecting

      • a. Young, immature believers

        2 Peter 2:18-19

      • c. The poor, disadvantaged, and desperate

        Mark 12:40

      • d. Weak, sinful people

        2 Timothy 3:6 2 Peter 2:14

    'for' - this verse explains why he feels comfortable urging them to watch out for false teachers. He warns them against the harm that would be done against the Gospel if their reputation for and pattern of obedience was destroyed by a false teacher.

    Knowing, believing, and obeying the truth of Scripture is your primary defense. The best way to identify counterfeit teaching is to know the genuine article; drowning yourself in listening to false teachers to find their errors will only cause you to know and fall into error yourself!

    Who is Lucius? We don't likely know.

16:25-27 All Glory to God

I. 25a The Gospel is God's Power

  • A. 1:16-17 To Save

    James 1:18 The Gospel is not just words: it is God's power. Do not be ashamed to share the Gospel, as the power of God is at work in it: either bringing death to those who reject it 2 Corinthians 2:16, or life to those who receive it.

  • B. To Strengthen

    'establish' - to cause to be inwardly firm, strengthen. Used by Paul to refer to making believers strong against error, temptation, and persecution.

    'according to my gospel' - this is the means by which the believer is not only saved, but then strengthened in that redeeming salvation.

II. 25b The Gospel is God's Gospel

'my gospel' - Galatians 1:11-12 The same Gospel received by the other apostles from God.

Not a human system or philosophy, nor the result of human investigation; it is wholly God's work and revelation.

  • A. His announcement to be believed

    Luke 2:10-11

  • B. His invitation to be accepted

    Isaiah 45:22; 55:1-2 Matthew 11:28 2 Corinthians 5:18-21

  • C. His command to be obeyed

    1:5; 15:18; 16:26 Mark 1:14-15 2 Thessalonians 1:8 1 Peter 4:17

    Refusal to obey will be judged. Accept His terms of surrender and receive a full and complete pardon.

III. 25c The Gospel is about God's Son

He Himself is the good news. 1:1-4 Jesus Christ the Lord's pre-existence, divine nature, birth, incarnation, life, death, resurrection, and future reign as Messiah are all pointed to by Paul as he overviews the Gospel: essential elements of genuine faith.

10 You must believe in these things, and act on them: relinquishing all personal sovereignty, acknowledging Christ eternally as your Teacher and Lord.

IV. 25d The Gospel is God's Eternal Plan

'mystery' - not the english word 'mystery', but rather something that God chooses not to reveal apart from revelation. Colossians 2:2 1:17 God's mystery is Christ Himself, and the Gospel Christ brought.

'long ages past' - before the Scripture revealed it. 2 Timothy 1:9 In eternity past, this was God's plan.

V. 26a The Gospel is God's Revelation

  • A. Through the Incarnation

    'but now' - at this point in time, in salavation history

    'manifested' - an event, a single disclosure: the incarnation of Christ 3:21

  • B. Through the OT Scripture

    'through the Scriptures of the prophets' - 1:2; 3:21 This refers to the Old Testament

    • Isaiah 61:1 The word 'gospel' finds itself used in several places in the OT septuagint.
    • Genesis 3:15; 12:7 Isaiah 53 In the beginning of human history
    • Luke 24:25-27; 44-47 Affirmed by Jesus after His resurrection
  • C. Through the Apostles and NT Scripture

    'has been fully made known' - Even the OT authors didn't fully understand, and were striving to understand the mystery. But now the mystery is made fully and completely known through Christ and His apostles.

    Christ repeatedly taught texts from the OT and fully and perfectly explained them. The Gospel has been revealed to you by God through His incarnated Son, the Old Testament He affirmed, and the New Testament He authorized.

VI. 26b The Gospel is God's Universal Message

cf. 1:5 'nations' - Grk. 'ethne', equiv. of Heb. 'goyim' - nations that do not believe in the one true God of Israel. God intended His Gospel to be proclaimed to all peoples.

There is no other Gospel, no other way. 'No one comes to the Father but through me.' Acts 4:12

VII. 26c The Gospel is God's Command

'leading to obedience of faith' - so that people of all nations may obey the Gospel, by repenting and believing it. It is not enough to know, understand, and agree that those facts are true. James points out that even demons know the basic facts of the Gospel, and tremble. It demands a response.

God commands you and me to repent and believe. Mark 1:14-15 Acts 17:30 Even a refusal to believe is itself an act of disobedience and rebellion.

This also leads to a pattern of obedience, brought by genuine faith. Matthew 28:19-20 Obedience is to the spiritual life what a pulse is to physical life: a vital sign. A lack of obedience or a lack of a pattern of obedience indicates you are spiritually dead.

VIII. 27 The Gospel is God's Glory

'the only wise God' - cf. 11:33-34 Paul, recounting the Gospel, marvels at the surpassing wisdom God displays in what He has brought about.

'through Jesus Christ' - God is most glorified through the work of His Son, Jesus Christ. God is the ultimate goal of the Gospel. Our rescue, justification, adoption, and eternal life with our Creator is not the ultimate goal, but rather His glory. Ephesians 1 repeats 'the praise of His glory' as Paul recounts the Bible.

'God is redeeming a people by His Son, for His Son, to His own glory.' - This is the story of Scripture.

'Amen' - a whole-hearted affirmation of these truths. 3:10,23,24-25; 4:5; 5:1; 6:23; 8:1,38-39; 16:27
