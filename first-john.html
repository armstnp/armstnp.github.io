'An Introduction to 1 John'

I. The Author: John the Apostle

While not explicitly stated in the epistle, it has been a unanimous consensus by the church that he was the author.

A. The Arguments for John the Apostle

Only modern skepticism has attempted to cast doubt on this claim.

  • 1. External Arguments
    • All the manuscripts identify 'John' as the writer of this letter.

      It does not specify which John.

    • The unanimous testimony of the church is that John wrote this letter.

      Irenaeus of Lyons, c. 185 AD; Clement of Alexandria, c. 200 AD; Tertullian, c. 200 AD; Origen of Alexandria, c. 240 AD; Eusebius, church historian c. 325 AD all held to this firmly.

      This understanding was requisite to the book being included in the canon of Scripture in the first place: it must have been written by an apostle, or one affirmed to write on behalf of an apostle.

  • 2. Internal Arguments
    • The author writes with the authority of an apostle. 4:6
    • The vocabulary and style of 1 John is very similar to John's Gospel.

      Examples: Heavy use of the word 'believe' but never/rarely the word 'faith'; very simple use of common ('koine') Greek.

    • The major themes of 1 John and are very similar to John's Gospel.
      • Common focus on Christ as being both human and divine
      • Common focus on love
      • The bulk of 1 John refers back to themes from the gospel of John, ~80% of the verses
    • There are similar phrases and expressions in both John's Gospel and 1 John.
      • 50+ parallels, often by the use of contrasts: light and dark, good and evil, truth and lies, love and hate
      • The new commandment to 'love one another'
    • The author's claim to have been an eyewitness of Jesus fits with John. 1:1-4

B. A Biographical Sketch of John the Apostle

Jesus chose James and John as two of His first disciples. Sons of Zebedee, they ran a large fishing business, and had good social standing in Capernaum and were well-known and respected in Jerusalem, and even by the high priest's house. John 18:15

Eventually the two took over the family business, forming a partnership with Simon Peter. Luke 5:10

Their mother, Salome, supported Jesus and the apostles. Luke 8:3 She was a woman of fervor and intensity, seen in part by her request to Jesus to allow her sons to rule alongside Christ in His kingdom at His left and right hand. She was also the sister of Jesus' mother mary, making James and John Jesus' first cousins.

Jesus nicknamed James and John 'boanerges', 'Sons of Thunder', which reflects their temperament as seen in the Gospel records.

Peter, James, and John became the inner circle of the apostles. James is always mentioned first in the NT, meaning he's probably the older, and was the first of the two to die.

John's name came from Hebrew 'Johanan' - 'God graciously gave'. He was first a disciple of John the Baptist first, then to Jesus. He was with Peter regularly throughout the Gospel accounts, and he was known as 'the disciple whom Jesus loved.' He was the only disciple with the women at the cross, and Jesus charged him with caring for His mother. He saw the resurrection, and was the first to believe in its reality. He was at the upper room at Pentecost 40 days later, and continued alongside Peter in the opening chapters of Acts. He became one of the pillars of the church in Jerusalem, and was one of those who vetted Paul as an apostle. Galatians 2:9

After the historical record concludes at the end of Acts, we have some evidence of what happened to John next from 2nd century sources: He left Israel shortly before 67 AD, when Paul was martyred in Rome, and three years before the Romans destroyed Jerusalem. He spent the next 30 years ministering in Asia Minor - modern-day Turkey - where Paul had planted so many churches. He eventually settled to lead in Ephesus, where Paul had spent three years, continuing the work Paul had begun, writing extensively.

Eventually, under the reign of Domitian, he was exiled to the isle of Patmos to work in the salt mines, and there he wrote the book of Revelation. He was later released, returned to Ephesus, and lived out his last days, died, and was buried there, the last apostle to die at the end of the first century. He was the only apostle to die a natural death. John's thunder was put towards the kingdom of Christ, newly tempered by love.

II. The Date and Audience of 1 John: 90-95 AD, to the Churches in Asia Minor.

The Gospel of John: 85-90 AD

Three Epistles: 90-95 AD

When John wrote 1 John, he was much older than his readers. 2:1,18,28

He doesn't refer to the persecution under Domitian which started in 95 AD, so that likely puts an upper bound on the year.

The false teaching John confronts arose in the last third of the first century, putting a lower bound on the year.

Revelation: 95-96 AD (near the end of Domitian's reign)

John wrote originally for the churches he served in Asia Minor during the final 30 years of his life.

The churches Christ wrote to in the beginning of Revelation, and the smaller churches in the cities and villages surrounding those major cities.

The epistle has no specific audience, meaning it was meant to be circulated among the churches along the postal route of Asia Minor, much as Revelation was..

John also wrote understanding that all believers would read and be benefited by his words.

cf. John 17:20

III. The Occasion of 1 John: Defection of False Teachers and their Followers

Paul had predicted that false teachers would arise within the Ephesian church, since it was the most important city in that region, and was the intellectual center. This made it a ripe target for false teaching to enter in.

2:18-19 John warns of the future Antichrist, but also of the present echoes of what is to come, those who reflect the character of that great evil ruler and his lies. In this passage, he makes clear that these false teachers ('antichrists') were a part of these churches, gathering followers, and eventually leaving the churches when those who stood for the truth held their ground. In leaving, they pulled away their followers with them and set out to gather more. 4:1

These false teachers left those churches, but continued to convince loyal followers of Christ to follow their bad example, bad theology, and immoral lifestyles. This mass exit of people from the church would have shaken the confidence of faithful teachers. 2:26

Who are the False Teachers in 1 John?

  • 1. Pre-Gnosticism?

    Not full-blown, but proto-version, coming after the Judaizers seen in e.g. Galatians. This was the most dangerous heresy to threaten the church during the first three centuries. It didn't reach its mature form until the second century, but it already existed in 'embryonic form' during the first century, as cited by Irenaeus, who said it was started by a man named Simon the Magician: the same seen in Acts 8. This should be taken with a grain of salt, but it is at least clear that it had its start before the 2nd century.

    This combined paganism syncretically - first with Judaism, and then with Christianity.

    • Matter is inherently evil, and spirit is good.

      False teachers were willing to affirm some degree of Jesus' deity, but they denied His humanity, since a fleshly body would taint Him with evil. This leads to the heresy of Docetism, from the Greek 'to seem' or 'to appear', claiming He only appeared to have a real body.

      Followers of this would sometimes treat their bodies harshly, a form of asceticism. Colossians 2

      Others would claim you could indulge in sin since it only affects the already-evil body and not the 'pure soul'.

    • A special few–the initiated–have a higher knowledge of truth, a knowledge that's gained mystically.

      This leads naturally to an attitude of superiority: 'out of step, uninformed, ignorant, not willing to progress'.

    • Salvation is escape from the body, accomplished not by faith in the work of Christ but by higher knowledge.

      This is not the Christian Gospel, not the Christian faith; there's mixing here of paganism and false Greek philosophy.

  • 2. Cerinthianism?

    Named after Cerinthus, a Jewish false teacher who lived in Ephesus at the time John was there. Irenaeus claims he taught the same false moral doctrine as the Nicolaitans, seen in the letters of Revelation.

    Polycarp tells a story of John going to the bathhouse, only to be told Cerinthus was already in there; John was reported to have fled without taking a bath, making clear that he believed God's judgment rested upon the man for distorting the Gospel, and that he would put himself at risk by even being under the same roof.

    • The 'spirit' of messiah descended on the human Jesus at his baptism and left him just before his crucifixion.

      This 'protects' the idea that you can't have God dying.

  • Heretics always claim to be 'progressives' with 'new ideas' and 'modern ways of thinking'.

    2:24

IV. The Purpose of 1 John

It's an epistle: even lacking many of the formalities of a 1st century letter, it says 'I write…' repeatedly, and the early church fathers all referred to it as such.

The shaken believers in the churches needed protection and reassurance, and John writes to give them those things, and to establish them more firmly in their faith by highlighting the differences between genuine and false believers.

A. Spiritual Protection 2:26-27; 3:7

The world is full of satanic deception about the Christian faith, and John tries to protect his readers from liars, deceivers, antichrists, children of the devil, and false prophets. He highlights the source of these lies as demonic.

This is not a polemic against the false teachers, and nor is he focused on contradicting error; instead, he focuses on reinforcing the understanding of the fundamentals of the faith.

B. Personal Assurance

  • 1. The Assurance of Eternal Life 1:3-4; 5:13

    This is giving assurance to those who already believe, where the Gospel of John was written so that its readers may receive eternal life.

    Assurance is vital for spiritual growth and effective ministry. God commands you to pursue it. And this assurance is found through Scripture alone, as the Spirit works through it to give you assurance. 2 Corinthians 13:5 2 Peter 1:10

    1 John 2:3; 3:14; 4:13; 5:13

  • 2. The Tests of Eternal Life

    Three tests recur throughout the book. If you examine your life and see these evidences, it should bring you joy! If not, repent and come to Christ! Throw yourself at His mercy and enter His rest of assurance.

    • a. Doctrinal: Do you believe in Jesus Christ and His Gospel?

      These things are required to be saved! You have to be putting your faith in what is true, and missing the mark on key elements of the Gospel will mean you are putting your faith in a lie.

      • His Person 2:22-23; 4:2-3,15; 5:1,5,10
      • His Work 1:7; 2:2; 3:5,16; 4:9-10,14; 5:11-12
    • b. Moral: Do you obey Jesus Christ and His Word? 1:5-10; 3:4-10,22-24; 4:5-6
    • c. Social: Do you love God and His people? 2:9,11; 3:10-15

Book Outline

John's structure is notoriously difficult to grasp, given the simplicity of its contents. Two mental images are that of a symphony, in which themes are woven together throughout various movements; and that of a spiral staircase, where the three tests presented as marks of a believer are reiterated.

1:1-4 Prologue - 'The Apostles' Proclamation'

I. 1-3 The Focus of the Apostles' Proclamation

1 Corinthians 1:23 2 Corinthians 4:5

John introduces several crucial facts about this person who is the center of their proclamation.

A. 1a He Existed Eternally.

'beginning' seems to indicate eternity past, given his use of the same phrase in 2:13-14.

There is also great similarity to the beginning of His Gospel. John 1:1 That in turn echoes the beginning of the whole Scripture, and of world history. Genesis 1:1

John 1:1 could be translated: 'In the beginning, the Word already was [continually]'.

God's eternality is regularly in view in Scripture: He existed before creation. Micah 5:2 John 17:5

This confronts the Cerenthian heresy head-on: Jesus existed before the incarnation and even the creation itself.

B. 1b He was Truly Human.

This attacks pre-gnosticism head-on, by refusing Docetism: that Jesus only seemed or appeared to have a real body. Instead, John retorts firmly that he was quite physically real. He gives the physical testimony of all the apostles, showing that with all their senses they had confirmed Christ's physical reality and His human nature.

This was no spiritual shared vision or hallucination. Luke 24:39

C. 1c He is God's Self-Expression.

'concerning the Word of Life' - the prior two clauses concern the Word of Life.

'Word of Life' refers most likely to Jesus Himself. John uses 'concerning' to describe persons cf. 5:9-10; the first clauses regard a person; the following verse continues on to describe it as a person; and John uses 'The Word' - Greek 'logos' - to refer to Christ.

'Logos' represented reason and rationality in Greek philosophy; but John drew his use of it from Hebrew Scripture, referring to the Word of God as His self-expression in creation Genesis 1:3 Psalm 33:6, and His self-expression in revelation Isaiah 38:4 John 1:18 Hebrews 1:1. Indeed, Christ is His ultimate and perfect revelatory self-expression.

[Personal note: 'He is the very image of God', Ephesians?]

D. 1d He is Self-existent and Gives Life to Everything and Everyone.

John - as he often does - has a dual meaning here.

John 1:4; 11:25; 14:6 has a similar phrase: God depends on nothing and no-one for life, being self-existent.

John 1:3 Through Christ, all physical beings receive physical life.

John 10:28 Through Christ, His own receive eternal life.

E. 2a He was Manifested in Human Flesh.

Verse 2 is a parentheses in the prologue, but it drives home the central theme of his Gospel: the Word was manifested.

'manifest' captures all of Jesus' life on Earth, the entire historical event of His incarnation. John 1:14 1 Timothy 3:16

F. 2b He was Eternally With God.

'which' - this Greek preposition can be interpreted, 'the one who - by His very nature - was with the Father' John 1:1

He is in person distinct from the Father; yet He enjoys an eternal relationship with God. He enjoyed a past continual existence with God. 'with' here means 'to' or 'toward', having a connotation of 'face-to-face with', i.e. an intimate relationship. Again, this echoes the beginning of John's Gospel.

He identifies Him as God's Son in verse 3.

G. 3b He is Jesus of Nazareth.

H. 3b He is the Promised Messiah and Savior.

Jesus is His name for a reason: 'Yeshua', 'YHWH Saves' - He is YHWH come in flesh to save. Matthew 1:21 4:14

Christ is His title for a reason: 'Messiah', He who would redeem His people as a propitiation, the blood sacrifice for their sins. Genesis 3:15 Isaiah 53 2:2

We can be saved from our sins in Christ alone. 3:23a; 5:10-12

II. 1-2 The Integrity of the Apostles' Proclamation

Legal witness has some strong conditions in order to be made a part of the legal record and foundation for a case. But testimonials are also the only type of evidence that does not need supporting evidence to be added to the record! Here, John gives his testimony alongside that of the other apostles.

A. 2 They were reporting historical events.

John 1:14 gives a similar accounting.

1 Timothy 3:16 is one of the earliest recorded confessions of the church, a compact statement of testified doctrine, and again speaking of the same things.

You can't write Jesus off as a myth or legend; you can call Him a liar, a fraud, etc.; but no credible historian believes he did not exist.

B. 1b-2 They were firsthand eyewitnesses.

Deuteronomy 17:6; 19:15 John 8:17-18 Two or three witnesses are required to affirm eyewitness testimonies.

2:1,7,8 show that John alone wrote this book ('I'), so John using 'we' here refers to the apostles as the corroborating eyewitnesses. 4:14

'we have heard' - Grk. indicates heard in the past, and continued to echo in their minds.

'we have seen with our eyes' - not a spiritual vision, but the sight of normal human physical senses. John - uniquely among the apostles - saw all the trials and the crucifixion himself. Again, Grk. indicates it lingers in his mind.

'looked at' - close scrutiny and inspection, analysis; there was no doubt as to Christ's physical reality.

'touched with our hands' - they touched the physical body of Jesus, verifying His physical reality, both before and after His crucifixion and resurrection! Jesus presented Himself to them to supply that crucial evidence after He had been raised.

This was 60 years later; how can he remember this so well?

  • The intensity of the experiences during the three years of Christ's ministry burned them into his memory.
  • John had been asked to recount this testimony day after day since they had occurred.
  • It was guaranteed by the work of the Holy Spirit in inspiration. John 14:25-26

C. 2b They were official witnesses.

'testify' - can refer to someone who steps forward as a witness, but also to someone who is chosen as a witness. Jesus chose the apostles as His hand-picked eyewitnesses. John 1:7 Luke 24:48 Acts 1:8; 2:32; 3:15; 10:38-41; 13:30-31; 26:16

Some read the testimony of the apostles and say, "I can't believe this is true;" yet we would not make a similar claim to any other event that occurred in history! We only know of historical events by eyewitness testimony; we only know of current events by eyewitness testimony! That which we have not seen, we only know by others' testimonies. Your confidence in those things is a factor of confidence in the eyewitnesses. You can't say you don't believe it because you didn't see it, but rather only that you don't trust Jesus or those he picked to be His witnesses.

D. 2b They were legal representatives.

'proclaim to you' - those who speak publicly, sometimes to public announcement by a legal representative. Acts 10:42

The word 'apostolos', from which we get 'apostle', was one chosen to be a legal representative. It literally means 'a sent one'. Luke 6:12-13 Galatians 1:1

They are authorized to act legally on behalf of another, much like delegating the power of attorney. Christ's apostles had been with Him for His entire ministry, seen all His works and heard all His teaching, and were equipped to represent Him (along with the Holy Spirit He had given them). John 15:27 1 John 1:5

Conclusion

If you reject the eyewitness testimony and the apostles, you are rejecting Christ Himself. This same principle applies for any other representative here on earth.

John 13:20; 20:19-20,24-31 If you believe the testimony of the apostles, you are blessed by God: you are saved. Christ wants you to accept the testimony of His hand-picked eyewitnesses.

III. 3-4 The Purpose of the Apostles' Proclamation

A. 3 The Assurance of our Fellowship

'so that' - purpose statement

Fellowship is not a mystical experience (emotional, sentimental feeling of 'God's presence'); since the nature of our fellowship with God is the same as our fellowship with other believers. And all believers have fellowship with God, regardless of how they feel.

This bad view of fellowship in modern times extends from the 'higher and deeper life' movement, which in part focused on 'feeling and experiencing God's presence'. These ideas are spiritually destructive.

Fellowship is not something subjective you feel, but something objective.

Fellowship - Grk. 'koinonia' - meaning common, referring to things held in common, such as co- ownership of a business or joint possessions of a married couple. See e.g. Luke 5:10. This implies a close, shared relationship, and used here it refers to such a relationship entered into at salvation.

At salvation, every believer becomes a child of God 3:1,10, and as children of God together, we have thus become brethren 3:14; 5:1 and have a close, loving relationship with these other children of God. Fellowship, then, is to have and to act on these new relationships.

  • 1. 3b With the apostles and all believers.

    Holding a common faith and a common interpersonal relationship with one another; when we believe the message of the apostles, we enter the same relationship with all other believers that the apostles shared with one another. 1 John 2:19

    Acts 2:41-42

    A genuine believer cannot remain unattached to a local church, a body of genuine believers; to do so is to sin against Christ, to ignore and to hate your brethren with whom you ought to have a bond of fellowship.

  • 2. 3c With the Father and His Son

    This is the foundation on which stands our fellowship with other believers.

    • a. We need to know we have a relationship with God as Father and His Son as Savior and Lord.

      'we have fellowship' - in Greek, this tense states that he is writing to those who certainly have it, not that might have it.

      2:3,5,12-14,23,27,29; 3:1,15,24; 4:7,12,13,15,16; 5:1,11,13,19,20

      This is the case for every believer. Fellowship is based on a fact, not on a feeling. You are never going to have consistent fellowship if you're searching for the right feeling, and expecting those feelings to remain constant; instead, we are enjoying the assurance of an objetive relationship we already have.

    • b. We need to grow in the depth of our relationship.

      2:12-14 shows stages of spiritual maturity, and the corresponding depth of relationship with the Father and the Son. Colossians 1:10 2 Peter 3:18

      This is not mystical and emotional, but rather based on faith in the Word of God. Feeling is not the engine of the Christian life, but the caboose; it follows the knowledge you have of God. You cannot grow without knowledge.

      Exodus 33:13-14 This is the second of Moses' three requests in this passage, asked 'since You have shown me grace'. God's 'ways' refer to the ruts of a cart path or walking path, as a metaphor for patterns of behaviour. God is not capricious in His behaviour, but is consistent, His actions able to be traced and followed (though certainly not fully). We grow in our relationship with Him by hearing His Word and seeing His works; we see His actions and thus come to know God.

      This is not mystical, ethereal communion founded on feelings, but based on concrete, objective actions. Consider the relationship of a parent with their child; learning progressively more and more about the character of the father as they grow, knowing them more personally as time and maturity progress.

      Emotional fumes will not sustain you. Only a genuine knowledge founded on an existing fellowship with God will.

B. 4 The Fulness of our Joy

'We write' - in solidarity with the other apostles, the all the others were - at this point - dead.

'so that' - his purpose in writing was for the joy of all the believers, which in turn would only increase his joy. This joy is joy enduring, based on an imperishable assurance 1 Peter. It is ultimately to be fulfilled in the life to come. Psalm 16:11

We have moved from eternity past, to the incarnation of Christ, and now into eternity future: a future of unending joy.

  • If you are born of God, get the mystical idea of fellowship out, and know that you are in fellowship.

1:5-2:27 The Tests of Eternal Life: Cycle 1

1:5-2:6 - Test 1: Obedience to Jesus Christ and His Word - 'The Believer's New Relationship to Sin'

I. 1:5 God's Essential Nature of Holiness

  • A. 5a The Unique Source of the Message
    • 1. It's from God's Son.

      'He has exegeted [God].'

    • 2. It's through Christ's Apostles.
  • B. 5b The Unique Content of the Message
    • 1. God is Holy.

      God is also described by John as spirit (not a physical being) and as love, and these three words are repeatedly used to describe God.

      John 17:11,25 shows how Christ addressed the Father, and clearly taught this to His apostles. Note that God does not have light or make light, but rather is Light.

      God's moral perfection and purity is in view here. He is separate or transcendent in majesty, and He is separate or transcendent in moral purity. And as one who is separate from anything sinful, He is separate from sinful mankind.

      • John's Use of Light and Darkness
        • 1. Intellectually

          Light is truth. Darkness is falsehood and error. cf. John 8:12

        • 2. Morally

          Light is moral purity or holiness. Darkness is evil and sin. cf. John 3:19-21

          This is what's being referred to here, given the continual parallel theme of sin.

    • 2. God is Completely without Sin.

      There is not even a fleck of darkness and moral evil - sin - to be found in Him at all. While the physical light we experience is always in degrees and shades, this statement further emphasizes God's holiness: it is not merely of the highest degree, but is so wholly separate that it has no shadow or shade. Habakkuk 1:13 James 1:13

      The Greek and other Gentile peoples thought of the gods as the Greek pantheon - evil, vindictive, petty, cruel, selfish, greedy, capricious, etc. But John makes clear that the character of God is perfect moral light; and thus, we must not live in sin ourselves.

II. 1:6-2:6 The Believer's New Relationship to Sin

'False Christians' - People who claim and profess to know Christ, but in fact have no relationship with God. John draws distinctions here between those who are genuine believers and those who make false professions.

A recurring pattern in this section: John quotes the false claim of a false Christian, and then responds with how a real Christian reacts. 'If we say <false claim> […] but if we <true claim>' etc.

  • A. 1:6-7 Shown by the Pattern of His Life
    • 1. 6 A false Christian habitually lives in sin.

      Proverbs 2:13; 4:18-19

      One who says they are a Christian, but live in a pattern of unbroken, unrepentant sin are not genuine believers. cf. 1 Corinthians 6:9-11 Galatians 5:19-21

      This claim may have risen from pre-gnosticism, claiming the body is inherently evil and so sin was excused as being inevitable. But even a vast number of American churches now hold this view without the undergirding of pre-gnosticism.

      A verbal profession of faith - praying a prayer, walking an aisle, throwing a stick in the fire at camp - is no guarantee of genuine saving faith! Those who work lawlessness as their way of life - those who disobey more than obey as a continuing pattern - should not trust in their profession as genuine. No amount of knowledge or profession of Christ's name can save in and of itself.

    • 2. 7 A true Christian habitually lives in holiness.

      1 Timothy 6:16 Because God's character is light, His actions are light - He lives in His own light.

      Colossians 1:13 Ephesians 5:8 As the Christian has a new life in Christ, He is to live accordingly.

      Having fellowship with other believers is evidence of having fellowship with God, as fellowship with God is prerequisite to fellowship with believers.

      'the blood of Jesus His Son' - the sacrificial death required as an atonement for sin.

      Cerinthus, the heretic, claimed that the man who died on the cross was only a man, and thus his death was not efficacious to cleanse sin.

      Walking in the light is not spiritual perfection or absence of sin 1:9-2:1. Rather, he's talking about what characterizes our lives.

      Isaiah 64:6 Titus 2:14 Christ's atoning death cleanses us by regeneration at the moment of salvation Acts 15:9 and by ongoing cleansins - sanctification - throughout our lives, by confession, repentance, and obedience 1:9.

      If the pattern of our lives is holiness, even if we do sin, Christ's blood will continue to cleanse us from all sin. A genuine Christian will have all their sin wholly dealt with.

      Ask yourself: what is the pattern of your life? Do you habitually live in sin, those things contrary to God's nature and Word? Or do you live in light, obedience to God's nature and Word?

  • B. 1:8-9 Shown by the Acceptance of His Inherent Sinfulness
    • 1. 8 A false Christian denies his sin nature.

      'If we are saying' - consistently claiming this, ongoing; the next couplet talk about actions of sin, but this one refers to a denial of sin nature: original sin, or total depravity.

      Original sin
      the effects of the sin of Adam on every person, consisting of the guilt of sin, and corruption in your nature from conception.
      Total depravity
      The comprehensive corruption of every part of our being: body, mind, and soul.

      The pre-gnostics were arguing as dualists: the body is evil, so you can do whatever you want with it. The spirit is inherently perfect, free of sin.

      The modern expression of denial of sin takes different forms:

      • "I am basically a good person who sometimes makes bad choices." - Liberal protestantism holds this, as does the average man on the street.
      • "I'm bad at times, but not totally bad; there's a part of me that's good."
      • "I'm not a good person, but I do some good things."

              1. You would have brought a physically perfect animal to the Temple.
              2. You would have laid your hands on the offering, with the implication that it was now your representative.
              3. You would have slaughtered it with your own hand.

Leviticus 1:9,13,17; 2:2,9; 4:31 and so on. This death calms God's just wrath against sin. The Greek uses 'the death' and 'the life' to emphasize these are the spiritual states of death and life. Romans 5:12 Ephesians 2:1 All are born spiritually dead, which results in our physical and eternal death in the Lake of Fire. We must needs be born again, given new life; we are now alive to God, conscious of and living in fellowship with God. John 5:24 is now true of John and those believers to whom he writes.

        How do we know this happened to us? '[…] because we love the brethren' - lit. 'because we are loving the brothers', a characteristic, continual pattern of love for our fellow believers. This does not earn us eternal life, but it provides evidence of that life. It is the primary mark of genuine saving faith, the firstfruit of the Spirit. 1 Corinthians 13:13 Galatians 5:22 Colossians 1:4 John 13:35

You show me a professing Christian who is content week-after-week, year-after-year to forsake fellowship with fellow believers, and I'll show you someone for whom we should carry grave concerns about their profession of faith.

        lit. 'The one not loving is remaining in death' - they remain in the 'country' of death, having never genuinely been born again.

    • 2. 15 Biblically Argued
      • a. If you are not consistently loving someone, you hate them.

        There is no middle ground, as we saw in 2:9-10. You either love, or you hate.

        What about the world's concept of indifference? That's not how God thinks! There's no neutral ground, because of the nature of God's moral law: it is not merely negative, but rather primarily positive. It tells us what we should do.

        Exodus 20:3-17 shows a condensation of the Ten Commandments: eight are negatively stated ('You shall not'), but two are positively stated ('You shall', indirectly). God's law also demands that we do the opposite of what each negative commands forbid: 'love and worship Him alone, from our hearts and only in the way He describes, keeping Him and all things related to Him holy.' 'Honor the human authorities in our lives.' 'Take every step to preserve our lives and the lives of others.' 'Enjoy sexual activity only within the bounds He has made.' etc.

        Matthew 22:36-40 Romans 13:8-10 Jesus and Paul alike pointed to the positive demand of the law as being its fulfillment, not just avoiding the negative demands. Love is the perfect fulfillment of the demands of the law, and anything less than that is hatred.

      • b. If you hate someone, you have broken the sixth commandment against murder in your heart.

        'murder' - the Greek word here is for 'mankiller', used also in John 8:44 to describe Satan's murderous heart that desires to see mankind butchered.

        God doesn't just demand the actions of His law, but that we carry the spirit of His laws in our hearts! We aren't to harbor murderous desire in our heart whilst never acting on it. That's not to say murder is not significantly worse, but rather that this heart attitude is still equally damning and makes you equally guilty before the law of God, equally guilty of eternal punishment.

        Matthew 5:21-22 Jesus equated anger in the heart with murderous intent! If you were prosecuted based on this evidence of your heart, you would be found guilty and would go into Hell. Your desire in that anger is that they would perish, and be out of your life: murder of the heart.

      • c. If you are characterized by patterns of anger and hatred (and thereby murder in the heart), you do not have eternal life.

        This does not mean you are beyond God's redemption, but rather that this pattern in present reality is evidence you have certainly not received that redemption yet. Galatians 5 Revelation 21:8

        If you believe you are a Christian, take stock and examine yourself: do you consistently love Christians? Then you have eternal life. Do you consistently fail to love Christians? Then you do not have eternal life.

        To fail to break even one commandment is to break the whole law; since the law is unified and demands perfect obedience, we cannot keep the rest if we have broken one, just like shattering a pane of glass.

        Christ lived a perfect life of righteousness, perfectly keeping the law of God. The only way for us to be made right with God is to put our faith in His perfect life, obedient death, and sealing resurrection. By this means alone is His righteousness credited to us. We follow Him, becoming His disciple and walking in His ways alone.

John 10:11-18; 15:13 His love for His people motivates His sacrificial actions, laying down His own life voluntarily to purchase our redemption.

IV. 19-24 Essential to the Believer's Assurance

      • a. 19b How? - We can use it to assure our heart.

        'we' and 'our' - plural - mixed with 'heart' - singular; all believers share this common experience, this 'same heart'.

        'assure' - this word can mean to 'convince' or 'persuade', but also to 'put at ease', 'pacify'. We can set our hearts' fears and misgivings to rest by this.

        This is a tranquilizer for the guilty conscience - not by suppressing it using your own mechansims, but rather by God's ordained means: by observing your own life in light of these tests God has given us regarding our own salvation.

        Our hearts know more about us than others, and thus can raise the alarm, declaring us guilty as it knows of even our secret sins and the guilt they ought to incur before holy God. Our conscience and our thoughts are made by God to condemn us and bring us to the reality of our state, our lack of and need for righteousness. The basic requirement of God's moral law is indelibly printed on every human heart. Romans 2:14-15

        The conscience is the courtroom of the soul, where moral decisions are constantly on trial. This court came 'pre-loaded' with the basic requirements of God's law; the conscience functions automatically, while our thoughts stand in the prosecution and defense of our own decisions. Both often accuse us.

        Even as believers, our hearts still condemn us at times because of our continuing sin, and we need assurance. This is not unsuual for believers; this is the norm! Multiple Confessions of Faith recognize that 'true believers may have their assurance shaken, diminished, or intermitted; by not being careful to preserve it, by falling into special sin that wounds the conscience, by facing vehement temptation, by God withdrawing the light of His countenance for a time; yet are they never utterly destitute of that seat of God, light of faith, that love of Christ and the brothers, the sincerity of the heart, conscience of duty, […]'

        So, given the reality of the condemnation of our hearts, we can assuage this condemnation by reminding ourselves of these tests, observing our own lives to point to it as evidence in this court that we belong to Christ.

        God understands the reality of our spiritual condition, and knows all things. Some say that John is saying 'God knows our sin, and it's worse than the condemnation of our hearts.' While this is true, it's not befitting the context: John isn't giving a warning here, and it certainly wouldn't give assurance! John is speaking here about assurance and confidence concerning your faith.

        • i. God knows that the condemnation of our hearts is sometimes false.

          We can appeal to the fact that God knows our hearts, our motives, and the genuineness of our love for Him. Psalm 103:13-14; 139:1-6 John 21:17

        • ii. God knows that He has forgiven the sins of which our hearts condemn us, and that we are still His children.

          My heart may condemn and accuse us for these sins, but His court has already declared us innocent by the blood of Christ. This was decreed not by our own merit, but by His good mercy and pleasure. By this alone we have hope.

        • iii. God knows that our active love for other believers proves we've been born again in spite of the sins that condemn us.

          If we have true fellowship with one another and with God, the blood of Christ continues cleansing us of all sins. He does not forget His saving intentions for each of us, but does not fail to remember and continue that work.

    • 3. 22b-c The Indispensable Conditions

      • Have faith in God's faithfulness to keep His promises and in His power to do what He hasn't promised if He chooses. Matthew 21:22 Mark 11:22-24
      • Be forgiven and have a spirit of forgiveness toward others. Mark 11:25

      Faith in the Gospel; Love for God and His People; and Obedience to Jesus Christ and His Word.

      John's point is that these three tests are the biblical means to gain assurance of salvation: if these three tests are how we know we have eternal life, then passing these tests provides us assurance, naturally.

      He returns to these tests, now combining all three together to make it clear that all three tests are required and unified, three legs of a single stool.

      • a. 23a Believe in Jesus Christ and His Gospel.

        'believe' - the first use of this word in this epistle; the saving faith and personal commitment to 'the name': the person of Jesus Christ and all that He is, including His being the Messiah. This expression is even phrased as a micro-creed, defying heresies that claimed Jesus was merely a man given divine power during the three years of his ministry.

        2. Believe that Jesus is fully man. 2:1; 3:3,7; 4:2-3 Matthew 1 Luke 1
        3. Believe that Jesus is the Christ. 2:22; 5:1
        4. Believe that Jesus is the only Savior. 2:1b-2; 4:9-10,14 Matthew 1:21 This also requires us understanding our own total depravity and hopeless state, requiring Christ as Savior.
        5. Repent of your sins and Confess Jesus as Lord. 3:4-8a; 5:1,10,13 (lit. 'into the name', i.e. in such a way that you become His and follow Him) John 8:31 Luke 6:46

        The Gospel is an announcement by God, an invitation from God, and a command to be obeyed by God; and failure to do so is just another expression of your rebellion against Him. 2 Corinthians 5:20 John 6:29

        If you have believe, taking and passing this first test can give you great assurance: rest in and trust in Christ and His promises. John 5:24; 10:27; 11:25-26

        This, however, is not the sole means of your assurance; all three tests are equally important, essential, and vital, as the others examine the evidence of the genuineness of this belief.

        cf. 2:10-11; 3:11,14 Faith and love are inseparably united; you cannot believe without loving other believers, and you cannot truly love without Christ. Galatians 5:6 1 Timothy 1:5

        Jesus commanded us to love, and modeled love for us by His life and death for us. 11,16

        Assurance, then, is not found solely in rehearsing our own belief in God, but examining the evidence of genuine faith in our life: the love for other believers given to us by God.

      • c. 24a Obey God's Word

        Obedience to God's commands is evidence you've been saved and that you possess eternal life.

        What commandments are referred to? His very commands to love God and love one another, that which Christ affirmed was the summary of all the law He gave. Romans 13:8-10 Galatians 5:14

        Believers live in a consistent pattern of obedience to God's Word - not in perfection, but in growing consistency. 2:3-5a

        Examine your life for these evidences and these changes, not trusting some prior commitment or experience alone!

        'abides' - 'to stay', 'to remain'; used throughout this epistle. 4:13,15 Genuine believers remain steadfastly in Christ, and Christ remains steadfastly in them. John 15:1-8 Christ is connected to the believer through the Holy Spirit, supplying them with life and fruit. Romans 8:9

        All three members of the Trinity are continually dwelling with the believer. John 14:23 Our obedience is evidence of His indwelling.

      Jesus and the Father both send the Spirit to indwell believers; a gift of God's grace to each. John 14-16 Romans 8-9

      When did God give us His Spirit?

      1. Historically at Pentecost Acts 2:1-4,22-24,33,36
      2. Personally at regeneration 1 John 3:24 Ezekiel 36:27 Romans 8:9

      1. He gives us a sense of internal confidence. Matthew 7 refutes this as being a primary means of assurance.
      2. He assures us through a mystical inner voice, speaking directly to the heart. But this would make this 'inner voice' more important than Scripture, and makes such assurance entirely subjective, and therefore uncertain. And we know the Spirit's pattern is to work through the Word.
      3. He assures us through the Scripture alone. 5:13

      The Holy Spirit's Role in Assurance

      1. He teaches us the biblical gospel and produces faith in us through our hearing and understanding the Word. 2:20,24,27; 4:2-3,6; 5:6-8
      2. He creates love in us for our fellow Christians. 4:12-13 cf. Galatians 5:16,22
      3. He produces righteousness in us by using the Word. 2:29 cf. 2 Corinthians 3:18

      The Spirit then uses the presence of these fruits He has produced through the Scripture to evidence our salvation. Whenever your heart condemns you, return to these evidences for assurance.

4:1-6 - Test 3: Faith in Jesus Christ and His Gospel - 'Recognizing False Teachers'

Edwards: The Distinguishing Marks of a Work of the Spirit of God

  • 1. Does it exalt the biblical Jesus and gospel? 2-3
  • 2. Does it promote true holiness? 4-5
  • 3. Does it exalt the Word of God? 6
  • 4. Does it promote selfless love for God and others? 7-21

I. 1 The Continual Danger of False Teachers

  • A. 1 Understand the Competing Versions of the Christian Faith

    'Beloved' - those beloved of God and John alike; those who have been saved by God, as evidenced by the tests John has been discussing.

    John is here to give a warning: behind every human teacher who claims to speak for God are spirit beings; and many who claim to speak for God are instead speaking demonic words: lies and distortions and corruptions of the Word of God; a counterfeit of the true faith, the true God, and the true Jesus.

    • 1. Originate from different sources

      John 8:40-47 From where are your spiritual ideas and concepts sourced? Where do they stem from, flow from? How do you evaluate those ideas that claim to be Christian? No idea is neutral; they either have as their source God, or Satan.

      • a. Genuine: 1 from God
      • b. Counterfeit: 4b 'he who is in the world' (Satan)
    • 2. Teach different messages
      • a. Genuine: 6 truth (the truth of God)
      • b. Counterfeit: 6 error (or error mixed with truth)
    • 3. Are empowered by different spirits

      'spirits' - the spirit beings who are behind and who empower the human teachers you hear. Only the Holy Spirit is acceptable as a genuine believer; all others are demons. 1 Timothy 4:1

      • a. Genuine: 2 The Spirit of God
      • b. Counterfeit: 3a every spirit that does not confess Jesus is not from God.
    • 4. Rely on different sources of revelation

      Ephesians 2:20 Prophets were provided in the NT before the canon was completed, not just in the OT.

      • a. Genuine: 1 from God (prophets from God); 6 'we are from God; he who knows God listens to us' (the apostles)
      • b. Counterfeit: 1 'many false prophets have gone out into the world'
    • 5. Embrace different fundamental doctrines
      • a. Genuine: 2-3 the biblical Jesus and the biblical Gospel
      • b. Counterfeit: 2-3 a false Jesus and false gospel
    • 6. Have different teachers
      • a. Genuine: 1 they are from God (true ministers)
      • b. Counterfeit: 5 'They are from the world; therefore they speak as from the world, and the world listens to them' (false ministers)
    • 7. Have different members
      • a. Genuine: 4 'You are from God'; 6 'He who knows God listens to us' (believers from God)
      • b. Counterfeit: 5b 'the world listens to them'; 6 'he who is not from God does not listen to us' (unbelievers from the world)
  • B. 1 Test Every Spiritual Idea against God's Word
    • 1. 1a Don't Be Foolishly Naive

      In the Grk: 'make it your consistent practice not to believe every human who claims to speak for God, assuming the spirit behind each teacher is the Holy Spirit'

      Satan is a master counterfeiter; don't be so foolish as to take all professing teachers at face value.

      John is giving all believers a warning to not be credulous; true faith examines its object before reposing confidence in it.

    • 2. 1b-c Be Spiritually Discerning

      'But' - strong adversative; 'but, on the other hand, instead'

      'test' - the same word used of testing for counterfeit coins and metals. This is not a cover for skepticism or cynicism, but of practical consideration and deliberation.

      This testing is required of every believer, not just the leadership of a church.

      • a. 1b The Duty

        1 Thessalonians 5:19-22 Examine, test, and hold fast to that which is true.

        You cannot test the spirit behind a teacher without evaluating the teacher themselves, and the content of what they teach. This evaluation will determine the source.

        This implies that we have an objective standard against which we are to test: that standard being the Word of God. Deuteronomy 13:1-5; 18:15-22 Even miracles performed cannot authenticate a message that conflicts with the rest of Scripture. Scripture is the highest measure. Acts 17:11

        Galatians 1:8-9 Believers have the right and responsibility to use the infallible rule of the Scripture to evaluate even teaching that claims to be from apostles and angels.

      • b. 1c The Reason

        'Many false prophets have gone out into the world' - evil spirits speaking through mankind, making the world their 'lecture hall'.

        Lit. 'pseudo-prophet', one who claims to be sent by God and to speak on behalf of God, when the words they speak are actually of Satan.

        False prophets were a problem throughout the history of Israel, and even in the New Testament era, and we are warned by Jesus of them. Matthew 7:15 Now, our problem isn't just false prophets, but false teachers: those who take the Word of God and twist it. Acts 20:29-30 2 Peter 2:1 Jude Revelation 2-3 2 Timothy 3:13 Matthew 24:11

        Jesus - our Lord - Paul, Jude, and John all warned about false prophets and teachers; and we see the fruit of that today. Receive their warnings and their charge of duty with all seriousness!

5:1-15 - Test 2: Faith in Jesus Christ and His Gospel - 'The Nature of Saving Faith'

There are kinds of faith - even 'attached' to the Christian faith - that do not save. To name some:

  • Natural 'faith': the ordinary trust you put in natural law and calculations of risk.
  • Historical 'faith': Intellectual assent to the historical verity of Scripture, without believing in its spiritual reality. James 2:19
  • Miraculous 'faith': Belief solely based on miracles done to or by them. John 2:23-25
  • Temporary 'faith': An initial eagerly emotional response to the gospel that is not genuine, and does not endure.

Only one kind of faith is saving faith: supernatural faith, which God gives at your spiritual rebirth. John expounds here on the nature of this saving faith.

I. 1a The Cause of Saving Faith

'Everyone who believes that Jesus is the Christ has been born of God'

  • A. A Summary of Saving Faith

    Lit. 'the one believing' - an individual exercising true saving faith, reflected in an ongoing reality of active belief.

    • 1. A Definition of Faith

      240 occurrences of this word in its verb and noun forms in the NT; central to it.

      Faith consists of three inseparable components:

      • Knowledge about the object of your belief; not a blind leap
      • Assent, internally convinced of the truthfulness of that knowledge; this includes acknowledging your own need
      • Trust in the object of your belief; acting on the knowledge to which you have assented in total dependent trust

      This faith is trust in the person of Christ, placing all our hope on Him alone for our salvation, rejecting ourselves utterly as contributing only our own damnation.

      John Murray - 'Faith is the whole-souled act of loving trust and self-commitment.'

      John MacArthur - 'Saving faith is a fundamental commitment of the whole person to the whole Christ.'

    • 2. The Content of Faith

      The Jesus Christ of history is the promised Messiah to Israel, He who would live a righteous life and die a death of substitutionary atonement; the eternal Son of God made flesh, who died, was buried, rose again, and ascended to Heaven; and who will return one day to reign and destroy His enemies and the reign and presence of sin once and for all.

      This cannot be believed out of our own power; you are not smarter or better than the rest of the people on this planet because you conjured the faith out of your own power! Rather, it is entirely given to us by God as His gracious gift. Philippians 1:29 Acts 16:14; 18:27 Ephesians 2:8

  • B. A Definition of a True Christian

    Lit. 'is born out of God'; i.e. 'is a true Christian', by our Lord's own words regarding the 'new birth'; also called 'regeneration'. This is the sovereign act of God by the Holy Spirit through the preached Gospel whereby He instantaneously breathes new life into a sinner in a moment in time, making one who was spiritually dead to now be alive.

    The mark of a true Christian is not what we have done, it is what God has done to us. It is a transformation of being, totally different than who one was before. 2 Corinthians 5:17 They know they are different than they were before, and different from those around them who are not saved. This changed nature should be evident to the believer, and indeed that is part of the reason for John's writing of this book.

    Note that a Christian is not someone who just tries to live a better life or to follow the ethics of Jesus, but one whom God has given new life.

  • C. The Role of the New Birth

    What is the relationship between faith and regeneration? John gives us some insight into the 'ordo saludis' - the 'order of salvation', the chronological order and logical/causal order of the events that occur in salvation. At the moment of salvation, six separate events happen to you at a single point in time.

    1. The Effectual Call: God calls you to Himself
    2. Regeneration: New life, the new birth
    3. Giving of Repentance and Faith
    4. Justification: Declared judicially right with God, crediting your sins to Christ and His righteousness to you
    5. Positional (or Definitive) Sanctification: God setting you apart to Himself
    6. Adoption: God adopting you as His own child

    Faith comes before and is the logical cause of Justification, for example, since we are 'justified by faith'.

    So what is the causal order between faith and the new birth? 'believing' is in the present tense, and 'born' is in the perfect tense, i.e. it has been completed in the past. John uses this construction in two other places to describe such causal relationships. 2:29 cf. Ezekiel 36:27 4:7

    John 1:12-13 Lit. 'The ones who are believing: these are the ones who, having been born, […]'

    John 3:3

    Apart from Christ, our moral inability means we can't even understand the gospel! We can't obey God or please Him; and we cannot come to Christ for salvation. 1 Corinthians 2:14 Romans 8:7-8 John 6:44

    So our faith is being described as being a consequence of the new birth: He makes us a new creature capable of faith, and then we believe. Ephesians 2:1-5 Without regeneration, it is morally and spiritually impossible for a person to believe in Christ; but in regeneration, it is morally and spiritually impossible for a person not to believe in Christ. We are born again by God in order to believe.

    … So why does this matter? It is, in fact, crucial.

  • D. The Practical Ramifications of the New Birth as the Cause of Faith
    • 1. It destroys our pride and produces humility and gratitude.

      Understanding that we could never have responded on our own, as we were dead men incapable of response, we know that we have endless gratitude toward God, because He alone could have given us life unto faith.

    • 2. It shapes our understanding of the entire doctrine of salvation.

      Our understanding of the reality of salvation exists, and is either more or less accurate to what is actually true. An incorrect understanding of the order of salvation inevitably produces a system by which man is the center of salvation, not God and not Christ.

    • 3. It affects our approach to evangelism.

      If you believe man is initiator of salvation, you will focus your efforts on persuading, encouraging, cajoling, convincing, and even manipulating people to believe; and you will ultimately use any technique to provoke that outward response of 'faith'.

      Consider the invitation or altar call, twenty rounds of 'Just As I Am', popularized by Charles Finney, who claimed that man was predisposed to accept the gospel and only needed to be manipulated emotionally into coming to that decision. Similar tactics are used today by churches teaching a false gospel.

      But if you believe God alone gives new birth through the teaching of His Word, then you'll teach the Word and leave the work in the heart to the Holy Spirit.

    • 4. It strengthens our peace and assurance.

      If I'm not responsible for my salvation, but God wholly is, what assurance! I don't have to worry that I did not do enough, because God is wholly able.

    • 5. It provides our hope of perseverance and ultimate glorification.

      Philippians 1:6 Since God begins the work of salvation, and not ourselves, we can be certain He will complete that same work. We didn't start it, and we can't end it prematurely.

    • 6. If you've never been born of God, it reduces you to a spiritual beggar, pleading with God for His grace.

      Since you cannot initiate salvation of your own will, you must come to God and plead before Him. All believers are those who have come as beggars before God, pleading with Him to save them from their piteous state. We have nothing God wants, and we cannot earn His favor; we can but ask Him to give grace.

    • 7. It gives God ultimate glory.

      We take no part of the credit or the glory for salvation on ourselves. No humility, no ostensible righteous living, not even a sliver of the last mile in salvation is ours. God is responsible for the whole, and He deserves all the glory, and we must give Him all the glory for His rescue of us! 1 Corinthians 1:26-31

II. 1b-5 The Results of Saving Faith

James 2:14-26 speaks of two kinds of faith in the church: real, living faith that saves; and deceiving, dead faith that damns. Both claim to be and are convinced they are christians; they embrace the same doctrine, claim the same Jesus and the same gospel; and both are present in every church, from James' day to this.

How do you identify it? James 2:26 tells us that it is known by the fruits which real, living, saving faith produces!

These results are guaranteed in the life of someone who has been born of God. Here, John brings all three of the tests together, showing their unity and coherence one with another.

  • A. 1b Love for God

    'everyone believing' - every individual who is a true christian is continually loving the Father. 4:19-20a The two are coupled, inherently the same thing. True saving faith always produces love for God; there are no exceptions.

    What does this love look like? How can you know if your love is genuine?

  • B. 1c Love for Other Believers

    This doesn't come easily or naturally to the flesh; and it can even be a challenge.

    Lit. 'everyone who loves the One who begats loves also the one who He has begotten' - this is not speaking of Jesus, though that love is certainly a prerequisite; but John is here speaking about your fellow christians who, like us, have been born of God. It is impossible to love God without loving His children. 2:10-11; 3:14; 4:20-21

    True christians love other christians because we see in them the same disposition as ourselves: we and they owe all to the grace of God; God sent Jesus to die for us and them alike; and we identify them as our spiritual siblings. We don't love them because we find them inherently likeable, but because we share the same Father, the same Lord, and the same redemption.

  • C. 2-3 Obedience to God's Word

    Loving God and loving our brothers and sisters in Christ each confirm one another: both must be present and in union. Both must be present in the christian life.

    Deliberate and consistent active obedience to God's moral law show us that we indeed have genuine faith. Genuine love for God expresses itself in obedience. So how does loving God and keeping His commandments prove that we love other christians? Because God's commands are summed up in those very things: 'Love God, and love your neighbor as your self.' Romans 13:8-10

    'For this is the love of God, that we keep His commandments' - the two are so connected, that loving God is defined here as keeping His commandments: it is the way in which we love God! 2 John 6 Our love for God causes us to desire to please Him. "If we judge our love for God purely at the emotional level without regard to the moral obedience which God's law demands, we might find ourselves excusing disobedience. […] This would cause us to find God's laws a bore, a chore, burdensome."

    Deuteronomy 5:10; 7:9; 10:12-13 God continually couples these things, and reminds us that His commands are always for our good! John 14:15 Christ Himself reaffirmed this - He did not break the commandments, but fulfilled them. Biblical love is always profoundly practical, and love for God is expressed practically in obedience. We know God's will for us, His command for us, by the revelation of His will that He has given to us in His Word.

    Before we were born of God, we saw His commandments as burdensome, restrictive, denying us happiness and joy; but having been saved and adopted by Him, we see His commandments aright as worthy of being treasured most highly, valued beyond measure, savored as the sweetest delight. His commands are not a crushing, exhausting burden; but in Christ, they are life-giving, meant for our well-being. Romans 7:22; 12:2

    Romans 8:7,10 Ezekiel 36 Before we were born again, we could not keep His commands, and so of course they were a crushing burden! But having been born again, He gives us the ability and desire to obey through Christ!

III. 6-12 The Object of Saving Faith

The kind of faith that saves believes certain testimony to be true. The object of your faith must be genuine and sure; anything other than the biblical Jesus cannot save you. Repent of any unworthy view of Jesus of Nazareth, and accept the testimony of those who witness regarding His nature and identity and work of redemption.

  • A. 6-9 God's Testimony that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God
    • 1. 6a-b The Statement of His Identity

      This is not a Jesus of our own making or perspective; rather He is the Son of God. Believers do not believe merely in God - other monotheistic faiths believe in a singular god - but we believe in particular in Jesus Christ, consubstantial and coequal with the Father, the God-Man - God incarnate.

      'who came' - 'the coming One', Messiah; except now He has already come!

      'Jesus Christ' - again, 'Jesus, the anointed one' or 'Jesus, Messiah'; He who would come to redeem man from sin.

      'by water and [by] blood' - three primary interpretations:

      • The water and blood that flowed from Jesus' side after He was pierced with a spear at the crucifixion (held by Augustine, but unlikely, given the qualification John makes in the latter half of this verse)
      • The two ordinances of the church: the water of baptism and the blood of the Lord's Table (held by the Reformers; unlikely, as this is referring to concrete events in our Lord's life, and the Lord's Table is never referred to as 'the blood' elsewhere)
      • Two key events in Jesus' earthly ministry: His baptism and death (Tertullian, and makes the most sense in context and in the framing of what John is now speaking)

      Jesus came to accomplish the redemption of His people, inaugurated for this purpose at baptism, and completing it by bringing it to its consummation at His death and resurrection.

      These two events confront the false teaching in these churches at Asia Minor: the Cerinthian gnosticism ravaging the churches in that age, which taught Jesus was merely human, and that the divine spirit of Messiah descended on Him at baptism and departed Him just before His crucifixion, meaning it was merely a man that died. But we believe He came to earth as an incarnate man in the womb of Mary, and continues to be the God-Man into eternity future, having taken to Himself full human nature, adding it to His divine nature - and this includes His death and resurrection. This Jesus is the object of our faith.

      What did the Cerinthians believe? This was merely a gifted man and teacher; but not God, and certainly incapable of saving mankind. To them, Jesus serves merely as a 'helpful guide'. And this is what the vast majority of people believe who hear of Him, and even the majority of churches and professing believers in the U.S. But He is not merely that, but the only way of salvation by which any man may be saved.

    • 2. 6c-9 The Evidence for His Identity
      • a. 6c-8 The First Three Witnesses
        • i. 6c The Spirit

          John 14 refers to this as well: the Spirit embodies the truth in a Person, and He testifies to these events as authenticating Jesus' nature, continually bearing witness to Him in His Word. The Spirit bears witness by and with the Word:

          1. John 1:29-34 The Spirit testified to Jesus' identity in His baptism by telling John the Baptist how to identify the Messiah.
          2. The Spirit testified to Jesus' identity in His death through:
            • John 19:19 Pilate's Inscription
            • Luke 23:2 John 19:7 The Testimony of the Jewish Leaders
            • Luke 23:42 The Testimony of the Repentant Thief
            • Mark 15:39 The Testimony of the Roman Soldiers
            • Romans 1:4 Jesus' Subsequent Resurrection
        • ii. 7-8 Two Events

          None of the early church fathers quote the first part of this verse; it's only present in four late manuscripts. Erasmus - the Roman Catholic scholar - excluded this from the first two editions of his Greek NT; under pressure, he agreed to include it if they could find a Greek manuscript including it. They found one… written that very year. So the added section appearing in the footnotes can be safely rejected as being part of the original text.

          John points to three witnesses that continually testify to the truth regarding God's Son, referring to the biblical standard of requiring two to three witnesses at minimum to authenticate testimony.

          Genesis 3:15 opens the first prophecy regarding the Messiah who would come to deal with sin.

          Isaiah 53 speaks of the mission of the Messiah and the means by which He would accomplish it: He would be sacrificed, becoming the perfect guilt offering for His people.

          Mark 1:4 shows John the Baptist, he who came before the Messiah to prepare the way for Him, baptizing those who demonstrated repentance for their sin: proselyte baptism, but not for Gentiles, but for the Jews! "You are no closer to the kingdom than the Gentiles; if you want to be part of the true Israel, you too need proselyte baptism."

          In light of this, Jesus' proselyte baptism - having to do with sin, repentance and forgiveness - is shocking. Yet it was not for His own sin, as He was perfect; but rather, He identified Himself with sinners, those whose sins He would eventually bear. This is the testimony of 'the water'.

          The testimony of 'the blood' is the testimony of Jesus' death. The OT prophesied that the Messiah would suffer, die, and be raised for the redemption of His people. Isaiah 53 Luke 24:44-48 Acts 17:1-3 This event of His death, therefore, testify to His being the rightful Messiah.

          'And the three are in agreement.' - These three witnesses all affirm the same thing, and are in perfect agreement, satisfying the measure of evidence God Himself required.

      • b. 9 The Ultimate Witness

        God Himself testifies to Jesus' identity; it's not merely humans that testify, but God Himself who testifies regarding His own Son. He is on the record in the New Testament stating that Jesus of Nazareth is His eternal Son.

        1. Matthew 3:17 At Jesus' Baptism
        2. Matthew 17:5 At Jesus' Transfiguration
        3. John 12:28 On Tuesday of the Passion Week
        4. Matthew 27:45,51-53 In the Miracles at the Cross

        How much more evidence is required? How much more loudly can God Himself proclaim it?

  • B. 10-12 God's Testimony that Eternal Life is a Gift of His Grace Received by Faith in His Son's Person and Work

    God testified about His Son during His ministry, both verbally and by miraculous events; and then gave testimony about those events in His Word.

    'has this witness in himself' - internalizing and accepting the truths of Scripture; having made God's testimony your own testimony; and having the Spirit of God affirming these truths in your heart, and affirming that you are truly God's child. Romans 8:16 Galatians 4:6

    • 1. 10 You must put your trust solely in Jesus Christ, the Son of God.

      Not just the facts of the Gospel, but rather 'believing into Jesus', as John often puts it.

      This is like the difference between believing that an airplane can fly and is safe; and actually getting on the plane and trusting that it will fly and carry you safely to your destination.

      To do otherwise is to reject God's own testimony concerning Jesus, effectively calling God a liar: an attack and assault on your Creator and Sustainer. Unbelief is a sin to be deplored, not merely a mistake to be pitied.

      This refers to a past act of conscious choice, not a passive ignorance: having heard the testimony of God, you have rejected it. Objections at this point are moral, not intellectual.

    • 2. 11-12 You must believe that eternal life is solely a gift of God's grace through His Son.

      'eternal life' - lit. 'the life belonging to the age', the life that characterizes the age to come. This refers both to duration and to kind or quality: "This is eternal life: that they may know You [Father], the only true God; and that they may know Me [Jesus Christ] whom You have sent."

      John 5:24

      • a. 11a Prompted by God's Grace Alone

        John 17:2 God gave Jesus authority to give His own eternal life. Romans 3:21-24 Every sinner who is made right with God is made righteous and given a right standing before God by the free gift of God's grace alone.

        2 Corinthians 11:7 2 Thessalonians 3:8 Revelation 21:6; 22:17 all use this term to refer to something without charge, without payment, without cost: a true gift, in which we earn no part of it - not by birth, by actions, by thoughts.

      • b. 11b Based on Christ's Work Alone

        John 5:26 Jesus earned the right to give us life through His work for us.

        i. 4:9 He became a man. ii. 3:5b,7b He obeyed God's law in sinless perfection. iii. 4:10 He died as a substitute for our justification. Romans 3:25 iv. He was raised from the dead in the resurrection.

        Based on these things, He can give us eternal life.

      • c. 12 Received by Faith Alone

        'has the Son' - as your Saviour and Lord; 'has the Father' - as your Father

        We have the Son as our Saviour and Lord by believing into Him. 'I am the life' - salvation is not found in a prayer or in a plan, but in a person.

      • 1. This passage is a test. If you have not believed in this gospel, and set your faith in the biblical Jesus, you are not a Christian.
      • 2. This passage is an invitation. If you have not believed, repent of your sins, and believe in this testimony regarding Jesus, and set your faith solely into Him.

        If you can see your faith is genuine becuase you see the results of the new birth in your life - a love for God, a love for His people, and obedience to His Word - then you may have assurance that you have eternal life in truth.

IV. 13-15 The Assurance of Saving Faith

It is possible to have a false assurance of salvation: self-deception. Matthew 7:21-23 The true confession of faith reveals itself in a life characterized by obedience; false confessions are revealed by life patterns of sin and disobedience.

It is also possible for genuine christians to have very weak assurance of salvation.

Assurance is crucial for our spiritual growth. "But who can tell how joyful this man was when he had gotten his roll again? For this roll was the assurance of his life and acceptance at the desired haven? O, how nimbly now did he go up the rest of the hill." - John Bunyan, Pilgrim's Progress. Faith saves us; assurance satisfies us.

  • A. 13 The Reality of Assurance
    • 1. It's confidence that we have eternal life, which includes confidence in our forgiveness, justification, adoption, and ultimate glorification.

      Biblical Doctrines: "The divinely-given confidence of the believer that he or she is truly saved."

      Evangelical Dictionary of Theology: "The confidence of the believer in Christ that notwithstanding his mortal sinful condition, he is irrevocably a child of God and an heir of heaven."

      Many downplay or reject the concept of assurance; including Roman Catholics, Arminians (can lose salvation), Wesleyans (perfectionists), Post-modernists (confidence in anything is arrogance), etc.

      John Stott: "It is common today to dismiss any claims to assurance of salvation as presumptuous, and to affirm that no certainty is possible this side of death; but certainty and humility do not exclude one another. If God's revealed purpose is not only that we should hear, believe, and live - but also that we should know - presumtuousness lies in doubting His word, not in trusting it."

      Romans 5:1-2 2 Timothy 1:12 1 John 2:3; 3:14; 4:13

    • 2. It's only for those who believe in the biblical Jesus and gospel.

      Lit. word order in the Greek: "These things I wrote to you in order that you may know that life you are having eternal to the ones believing into the name of the Son of God." - John clarifies the qualifications for his audience.

      2:19,24 A profession of faith in the biblical gospel and biblical Jesus in the past is insufficient: this is ongoing, continual belief and all it resulting effects.

    • 3. It's not equally enjoyed by all true Christians.

      The youngest Christian understands their sins are forgiven by God through Christ. But John is writing to those who believe to give them assurance that they must be lacking, in part hampered by the false claims of the gnostics.

      What are some things that can cloud our vision and erode our assurance?

      • Temptation, unrepentant sin, and sinful habits ("Would any real Christian have…?")
      • Neglect of the spiritual disciplines, especially the reading of God's Word and prayer
      • Ignorance of or failure to personally apply the doctrine of justification Romans 3-8
      • Feelings of guilt about past sins
      • Uncertainty about the timing of one's conversion, or having been saved as a child
      • The influence of other professing Christians (both by comparison with great saints who came before, or by unsettling false teachings)
      • Physical exhaustion or illness
    • 4. It's God's explicit plan for every believer.

      God desires His children to have His assurance of salvation! Hebrews 6:17-18

      God also commands us to pursue assurance; it's not optional. 2 Corinthians 13:5 2 Peter 1:10 This is the primary purpose for which John has written this letter!

    • 5. It's primarily objective, not subjective.

      Grk. 'oida' - expresses knowledge characterized by assurance, something known with certainty. e.g. Romans 8:28 This is in direct contrast to what you feel - this is not based on your emotions, a terrible way to measure your assurance of salvation.

      This is a full conviction grounded on the certainties of Scripture - the emotions should follow that of which you are certain, not the other way around.

    • 6. It's gained by applying the three tests of eternal life in 1 John.

      What are wrong means to seek assurance?

      • Religious ritual (e.g. the Catholic sacraments; in a prior baptism)
      • In assurance itself ("I just know I'm a Christian"; empty circular reasoning Deuteronomy 29:19)
      • In the testimony of others ("I just know you're a Christian", often cited by family and well-meaning parents)
      • In your own experiences ("I had a vision of Jesus," "I had a near-death experience," "I prayed the sinner's prayer/walked an aisle/etc.")

      What are the right means to seek assurance? The contents of the entire letter, most likely, given John uses this structure to refer to his reason for writing his Gospel record, as well. 1:4 - 'is writing', and now 'has written': these statements bracket the entire letter in a way. Even if it is the proximate context of this chapter, that chapter covers the same three tests anyway.

      • a. Examine your life against the three tests in 1 John.

        Love for God and His People, Faith in Jesus Christ and His Gospel, Obedience to Christ and His Word.

      • b. Believe and rest in the promises of the gospel.

        Having observed the God-given evidences of salvation, trust wholly in the promises God has given to you, using Scripture to wage war against those sinful doubts that arise in your mind; such verses as John 3:16; 5:24; 6:40; 10:27-29; 11:25-26 Acts 10:43 Romans 8:29-30.

  • B. 14-15 A Result of Assurance - Confidence in Prayer

      'confidence' - lit. 'all-speech'; frankness or outspokenness, concealing nothing; or boldness or confidence, in the presence of someone of infinitely higher rank.

      'before Him' - lit. 'toward' or 'with', to be near or face someone in active fellowship; we are in an intimate, face-to-face relationship with God as Father, and can speak boldly within the context of this relationship without any presumptuousness. Indeed, God wants us to have this confidence. 3:22; 4:17 Ephesians 3:12 Hebrews 4:16 Would He refuse His own Son any good thing? How could He then refuse to hear us, whom He loves now with this same love?

Matthew 12:36 Psalm 139:2 1 Samuel 1:13 But God is not obligated to hear and respond to any but the repentant prayer of an unbeliever. Luke 18:13-14 Proverbs 28:9 1 Peter 3:12 He always listens with love to the prayers of His children. Psalm 34:15-18

        • i. 14b He will hear us with favor.

        • ii. 15 He will grant our requests.

          Because our prayers - under those conditions - are according to His will, He delights in granting them!

          Daniel 10 God does not delay in His response to prayer; even if the result takes time to work out in our lives and in our understanding - or even if we don't understand it here on this earth - His answer to our prayer is instant.

          Knowing this, go through the Bible and make a list of God's promises to you, and take those to God, pleading them in His presence, and you may be certain that God grants it to you in full. He will withhold from you nothing He has already purchased for you and desires for you to have out of His love for you.

5:16-21 - Test 3: Obedience to Jesus Christ and His Word - 'Real Christians and Deepfakes'

What is the focus of this section?

The third test, which John has not covered yet in this circle; this is affirmed by his focus on sin and obedience, especially as regards the first half of the ten commandments focusing on man's relationship to God, instead of man's relationship to one another as he had in prior cycles.

I. 16-17 Praying for Those in False Forms of the Faith or with False Professions of the True Faith

'sin not leading to death', 'sin leading to death' - What are these? What does this mean?

Two common views:

  • Praying for true christians
  • Praying for false christians (someone who professes to know Jesus but who is not a true christian, either believing in a false Jesus and false gospel, or not having genuine belief in the true Jesus and true gospel.
  • A. View One: Pray for the restoration of a true believer living in sin.

    While this may not be what John means here, this is certainly in accordance with the rest of Scripture.

    • 1. The Argument

      'his brother' - used throughout the book to refer to fellow christians.

    • 2. 16a The Circumstances

      John has just spoken of access in prayer we have to God; and now he gives a concrete example.

      'If anyone sees' - any christian witnessing this occurrence or has reasonable evidence

      'a sin not leading to death' - unreprentant sin in the life of a believer may lead to divine discipline, which may include physical illness and even death. e.g., Acts 5:1-11 1 Corinthians 11:27-30 So this view takes this to mean patterns of unrepentant sin in the life of a true believer.

    • 3. 16b The Command

      'he shall ask' - praying for God to intercede in the life of that person, bringing them to repentance and restoration. Psalm 23:3

    • 4. 16c The Answer

      'God will for him give life to those who commit sin not leading to death' - not eternal life, but temporal life.

    • 5. 16d The Exception

      Not the Catholic concept of 'mortal sin' vs. 'venial sin'; nor is it 'the unpardonable sin', which is intentionally attributing the works of God to be the work of Satan, something likely only possible during the time of Jesus, and those who attributed His works to Satan.

      This gives us two alternatives, both of which involve knowing the truth, and then knowingly defying the truth in a sustained pattern of life:

      • a. Don't pray for a true believer living in a pattern of sin that may lead to physical death.

        1 Corinthians 5:5; 11:30

        Premeditated or unconfessed sin that naturally leads to death as its end, high-handed sin, may cause God to permit that death for the purity of His church.

      • b. Don't pray for a false believer or teacher who has abandoned the genuine faith.

        Jude 13 Hebrews 10:26

    • 6. 17 A Reminder

      While it is true there are sins that don't lead to physical death, all sin is still serious, and is not to be trifled with.

  • B. View Two: Pray for the salvation of a false believer living in deception.

    Tom believes this to be the view:

    • Death must be eternal death and not physical death, because it's positioned opposite to the word for eternal life; and he's used these words in this way only throughout this whole epistle.
    • 'brother' only receives eternal life after believers pray for him.
    • The rest of this paragraph and book points to those who left their churches following false teachers; those who 'went out from us because they were not of us'.
    • 1. The Arguments

      'his brother' - also used by John to refer to false christians. cf. 3:17

      God responds to the prayer for this person by giving them life (Grk. 'zoe', eternal life), implying they did not have eternal life before.

      The theme of this paragraph as a whole is not about a believer's sins, but about what they believe vs. the error of what the world believes: real vs. false christianity.

    • 2. 16a The Circumstances
      • a. A professing believer has embraced a false form of the Christian faith.
      • b. A professing believer has demonstrated a false profession of faith in biblical Christianity.
    • 3. 16b The Command

      We are to pray for God to remove their spiritual blindness, praying for their salvation, and going to them and pleading for them to turn, 'snatching them from the fire'.

    • 4. 16c The Answer

      Pray to God for their salvation, and God will save them - not an ironclad promise God will save every unbeliever we pray for Romans 9, but rather that He hears your prayer, and the God who delights to save will save those He has chosen from eternity past in answer to your prayer.

      We are to pray for these, and to persist in prayer. 1 Timothy 1:13-16

    • 5. 16d The Exception: False Teachers

      Pray for the individuals who have been deceived, not for those doing the deceiving: those who separate themselves from the true church, rejecting and perverting the gospel. These ones' doom is sure. 2 Peter 2:3b,9,17 Jude 13

      We don't know whom God has chosen; this isn't an absolute command, and we can pray for their salvation. But such prayers may likely not be effective, as God has already consigned many such ones to the darkness.

II. 18-21 Distinguishing Real Christians from False Christians

Three Christian certainties that real Christians know, and that distinguish real Christians from false 'christians'; and these help us judge the validity of our own profession.

  • A. 18 Real Christians Have a New Relationship with Sin.
    • 1. 18a The Reality

      'no one' - about individuals; without exception.

      'who has experienced the new birth' - This is not a passing change, but a permanent, fundamental one.

      'is sinning' - as seen before when John used this term: this does not refer to a state of sinless perfection. 1:8-10; 2:1 We still have a sin nature and commit acts of sin, and we are to live in a pattern of confession of and repentance over our sin, and to be in a continual war against our sin. Ongoing, unrepentant sin is not, however, a lifelong pattern any more. 1 John 2:3-4; 3:4-10 2 Peter 2:22 Loving, wallowing in, content with, and spending most of your time in your sin shows you are not a Christian, not born of God; hating, fleeing, loathing, and desiring to be clean of sin evidences you are a Christian, born of God.

    • 2. 18b The Reason

      'He who was begotten of God keeps him' - the verb tense changes; not 'the one having been born', used to refer to Christians, but 'the one born', referring to a continual state of being, the eternal Sonship of Christ. 1 John 4:9

      'keeps' - 'holding onto something, so as not to give it up or lose it'; He continually holds on to, protects, and preserves every believer. While Christians are called to persevere in our faith, but the ultimate reason Christians do not live in patterns of ongoing unrepentant sin is because Christ protects them.

      From what does Christ protect us?

      • From enslaving patterns of unrepentant sin 5:18 Romans 6:1-2,14
      • From Satan's damning lies 2:20-21,27 Jude 24-25
      • From perishing forever John 17:12

      How does Christ protect us?

      • His revealed Word to us 2 Timothy 3:16; 4:2
      • His Spirit's work in us 3:9
      • His continual intercession for us 2:1 Luke 22:31-32 John 17:7,24 Romans 8:33-34 Hebrews 2:18; 4:14-15 For our protection, our sanctification, for our endurance and aid through trial and temptation, and for our eventual glorification
    • 3. 18c The Result

      'touch' - to contact in order to cause harm; Satan cannot drag us back into our former life of sin. The devil will fail in his efforts to recapture us. John 10:28

  • B. 19 Real Christians Have a New Father and Family.
    • 1. A New Father - 'of God' vs. 'in the evil one'

      Lit. 'out of God'

      'the evil one' - Satan governs this world; this world's freedom is slavery, and his promise of autonomy from God is an illusion. If you are not under the control of God, you are under the control of Satan, whether you are aware of it or not. He is the father of all unbelievers ('You are of your father, the devil'), and he gives his children his nature, enslaving them to sin just as he already is. He implants in his children a desire to think like he thinks and to rebel as he has rebeled. And he blinds unbelievers to the truths of the gospel. 2 Corinthians 4:4

      Satan's primary blinding strategies:

      • He promotes secular human philosophy. 1 Corinthians 2:6,12 2 Corinthians 10:3-5 James 3:13-16
      • He invents and promotes false religion. Deuteronomy 32:16-17 Acts 13:6-10 1 Corinthians 10:20
      • He corrupts the true gospel and distorts the true Christ. John 8:39-45 1 Corinthians 12:3 2 Corinthians 11:13-15 1 Timothy 4:1-3 1 John 4:2-3 2 John 7
      • He produces false believers. Matthew 7:21-23; 13:36-39

      Satan - an unspeakably evil person - dominates the minds of this world, and we must be careful about who we listen to, because if the voices we learn from are not from God, they are from Satan.

    • 2. A New Family - 'We' vs. 'the whole world'

      God's family, now, in contrast to Satan's family. We used to belong with unbelievers, feeling perfectly comfortable with them, sharing their priorities and values, loving what they love; but now, having been adopted into the family of God, we now love God's people, our new family; and we love the things that mark this family, and hate those things that mark our old family.

      This adoption cannot be undone; our emancipation is permanent, as God will certainly keep us. Our old, cruel father can no longer touch us or take us back.

  • C. 20-21 Real Christians Have a New Saving Understanding of the True God
    • 1. 20a Its Source - The Son of God Incarnate

      1:1-3; 4:2

      'has given' - given at a point in the past, and that He continues to give ongoing.

      'understanding' - the capacity to discern true reality

      John 8:46-47 Those not of God do not have a capacity to understand the words of God: they cannot discern spiritually.

      • a. Through His Word - Revelation
        • i. By explaining God's previous revelation

          Matthew 5:17-20 Mark 1:37-38 Luke 24:44-48

          Jesus' primary ministry during those three years was preaching and explaining the Old Testament scriptures.

        • ii. By bringing God's new revelation

          John 12:49; 17:7-8

          Jesus was, indeed, a prophet.

        • iii. By authorizing the NT revelation

          John 14:25-26; 15:27; 16:12-13; 17:20 Acts 1:8; 10:39-42 Colossians 4:16 1 Thessalonians 5:27 1 Timothy 5:18 2 Peter 3:16 1 John 1:2; 4:6

          He selected the apostles, who would represent Him on His behalf, bearing His authority.

      • b. Through His Spirit
        • i. By giving us spiritual life - Regeneration

          1 Corinthians 2:14

        • ii. By giving us spiritual insight - Illumination

          1 Corinthians 2:15

          'he who is spiritual' - those who have been born again to spiritual life

    • 2. 20b Its Result
      • a. We know the true God.

        In contrast to false idols, Jesus is genuinely the incarnate Son of God, and Yahweh whom He taught is genuinely God - the God of the Old Testament. 4:9-10

        He came that you may have a true relationship with Him as your Father. John 17:2-3

        Reconciliation with, true knowledge of, and personal relationship with God 2 Corinthians 5:21

      • b. We are in the true God.
        • i. We are united to Him and share His life.

          Ephesians 4:18 We are included in the life of God Himself.

        • ii. We have continuing fellowship with Him.

          1:3

          Because we are in the Son and have continuing relationship with Him, we also are in the Father and have a continuing relationship with Him. It is only in our mediator, Christ, that this relationship is possible.

    • 3. 20c Its Exclusivity

      'This is' - what is 'this'? It could refer to the Father, given context and prior usage; it could refer to Jesus, the nearest antecedent grammatically, and John has referred to Jesus as the eternal life previously; or God generally, the God of Scripture, wrapping up all He has discussed in a single referent. This seems likely.

      The God of Scripture is the Way, the Truth, and the Life. John 14:6; 17:3 Acts 4:12 Jesus taught us that the Christian faith is exclusive, and that no other claim to deity and to eternal life is true. Jesus' life, death, and resurrection are exclusively the path to salvation.

    • 4. 21 Its Enemy

      'Little children' - John's favorite term of affection for fellow believers

      'guard' - to stand in defense of

      lit. 'guard yourselves from the idols' - Idols of the heart Ezekiel 14:4, the 'gods' of false religion 1 Thessalonians 1:9, and false understandings of the true God Deuteronomy 5:8; which is referred to, here? Probably not the pagan idolatry of the surrounding culture, but rather the idolatrous false christianity John has been confronting this entire book. All false claims that distort the Scripture; all false human conceptions of who Jesus is; all these are false.

      Anyone who teaches anything else about God, Jesus, the Gospel, and Scripture than what Jesus Himself taught is false, and their teaching puts your spiritual health in great peril! We are to guard ourselves from listening to and believing such a one. Consider Roman Catholic attacks on grace; the libertinism of licentiousness; God stripped of His supernatural nature; overfamiliarity cloaking His supremacy; any and all of these pose great danger to us and to those we disciple.

    • The Overarching Lessons
      • 1. A call for repentance and faith, if you don't claim to be a follower of Jesus.

        This is the only way you will be forgiven and reconciled with the God against Whom you have horribly sinned.

      • 2. A call for self-examination, if you claim to be a follower of Jesus.
      • 3. A call for assurance, if you pass the three tests of eternal life.
